## Scope

Chapter 8 presents **human-centered AI (HCAI)** as the oversight layer completing the book’s **SAFE-HAI** framework (Sustainable, Accurate, Fair, Explainable AI under human control). It maps EU AI Act and NIST AI RMF human-oversight requirements to a three-step operational procedure—evaluate metrics, assess risk, improve models—and walks a full **bitcoin price-direction prediction** case study from baseline feed-forward neural network through recurrent alternatives (NNAR, LSTM, GRU).

**Sections ingested:** HCAI / human-in-the-loop framing · Regulatory anchors (EU AI Act, NIST RMF) · Three-step oversight procedure · Evaluating AI (bitcoin FFNN + SAFE metrics) · Assessing AI (probability × severity risk) · Improving AI (NNAR/LSTM/GRU comparison) · Scoring rubric (Table 8-1) · Mitigation · Chapter summary.

Cross-refs: Chapter 7 (sustainability/ESG) · Chapters 2–5 (RGA/RGR/RGE/RGF metrics) · Chapter 10 (second oversight case study) · Giudici & Raffinetti 2023 (financial predictions use case) · Giudici & Abu-Hashish 2021 (bitcoin data).

## Key findings

All claims **[verified from text]** unless tagged `[contested in chapter]`.

### HCAI and regulatory framing

- AI systems should be **tools used and overseen by humans** (“human in the loop” / human oversight). [verified from text, L4739–4742]
- **EU AI Act:** high-risk systems must allow effective oversight by natural persons during use to prevent/minimize health, safety, and fundamental-rights risks. [verified from text, L4744–4748]
- **NIST AI RMF:** risk management drives responsible use by prompting critical thinking about context and impacts; establishes correspondence between oversight and risk management. [verified from text, L4750–4757]
- **SAFE-HAI** = SAFE metrics + human oversight; chapter demonstrates evaluation/assessment/improvement on a financial ML application. [verified from text, L4759–4793]

### Three-step human oversight procedure

1. **Evaluate:** adapt SAFE-HAI to model outputs → compliance metrics (RGA, RGR, RGE, RGF). [verified from text, L4774–4777]
2. **Assess:** translate metric results into risk management (probability × severity). [verified from text, L4781–4784]
3. **Improve:** implement actions including new models to raise compliance. [verified from text, L4788–4792]
- Second oversight use case deferred to Chapter 10. [verified from text, L4796–4797]

### Evaluating AI — bitcoin baseline model

- Data: **Coinbase bitcoin** daily direction (up/down); explanatory series Oil, Gold, S&P500, USD/Yuan, USD/Eur (Giudici & Abu-Hashish 2021); May 18, 2016–Apr 30, 2018; 2018 test set. [verified from text, L4801–4820]
- Model: **feed-forward NN**, one hidden layer, five hidden units; percentage returns for stationarity. [verified from text, L4822–4828]
- SAFE metrics on baseline: **RGA = 55%** (low accuracy, expected for speculative asset); **RGR = 81%** (relatively robust); **RGE:** Gold 17%, Oil 6%, USD/Yuan 3%, S&P 3%, USD/Eur 2%; **RGF = 71%** with protected variable = high vs low **trading volume** (vs daily mean). `[contested in chapter]` — trading volume as fairness protected attribute is unconventional vs demographic groups. [verified from text, L4835–4856]
- Statistical tests: accuracy **not** significantly above random; robustness **is** significant; **Gold only** significant explanatory variable; fairness **not** significantly fair. [verified from text, L4858–4861]
- Narrative: model partially explainable via “digital gold” story linking bitcoin to gold prices. [verified from text, L4871–4874]

### Assessing AI — risk decomposition

- Harm risk = **probability × severity** (product or convolution). [verified from text, L4880–4882]
- **Probability mapping:** complement of metric value — e.g. RGA 55% → P(not accurate) 45%; RGR 81% → 19%; RGF 71% → 29%; sum of RGE 31% → P(not explainable) 69%. `[contested in chapter]` — linear complement mapping lacks calibrated justification. [verified from text, L4884–4895]
- **Severity** (ordinal low/medium/high) requires harm taxonomy and stakeholders (Abercrombie et al. 2024). For bitcoin predictor: low accuracy → **high** severity (financial loss); low explainability → **low** severity (bitcoin opacity known); low robustness → high; low fairness → limited (technical asset). [verified from text, L4897–4914]
- Dominant risk: **accuracy (model risk)**; recommendation: replace model. [verified from text, L4915–4919]

### Improving AI — recurrent networks

- Baseline FFNN assumes **day independence**; bitcoin decisions often use **past bitcoin prices**. [verified from text, L4929–4937]
- Alternatives: **NNAR** (p lagged inputs + hidden layer), **LSTM**, **GRU** (fewer computations than LSTM). [verified from text, L4940–5001]
- **Accuracy (RGA):** NNAR 0.3718 (worse); LSTM 0.8186; GRU **0.8865** (baseline 0.55). NNAR overfits with too many inputs; LSTM/GRU more parsimonious. [verified from text, L5011–5021]
- **Robustness (RGR):** NNAR 0.7157 (poor); LSTM **0.9607**; GRU 0.9244 (baseline 0.81). [verified from text, L5023–5029]
- **Explainability (ΣRGE):** baseline ~31%; NNAR 0.63; LSTM 0.56; GRU 0.62. [verified from text, L5031–5036]
- **Model choice:** prioritize **GRU** for accuracy (primary risk); LSTM trades slight accuracy/explainability for higher robustness. Human oversight conclusion: replace FFNN with **gated recurrent network**. [verified from text, L5038–5048]

### Scoring rubric and mitigation

- **Table 8-1:** improvement ratio (new vs baseline metric delta / baseline) — Excellent 50–100%, Good 30–50%, Fair 20–30%, Borderline 10–20%, Poor 0–10%; values exemplificatory per risk appetite. [verified from text, L5050–5067]
- Mitigation: increase data and/or change model/algorithm; chapter demonstrated architecture change for accuracy. [verified from text, L5069–5077]
