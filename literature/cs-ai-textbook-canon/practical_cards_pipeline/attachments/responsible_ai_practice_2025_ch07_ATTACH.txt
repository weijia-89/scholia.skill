## Scope

Chapter 7 extends the book’s SAFE metrics family to **sustainability**, operationalized through **environmental, social, and governance (ESG)** criteria and their embedding in ML models for finance-sector case studies. The chapter bridges regulatory framing (EU AI Act energy-consumption considerations for systemic-risk GPAI; UN AI Advisory Board SDG alignment) with empirical European corporate-finance examples and introduces **Rank Graduation Sustainability (RGS)** as a model-level metric parallel to RGE (Chapter 4).

**Sections ingested:** ESG definition and sustainable finance · E/S/G dimension features · Environmental sustainability (Agosto et al. 2023 Bayesian ensemble) · Social and governance sustainability (Bosone et al. 2022 gender diversity) · Model sustainability (RGS formula) · Scoring rubric (Table 7-1) · Mitigation (social, economic, environmental) · Chapter summary.

Cross-refs: Chapter 6 (RGP privacy) · Chapter 4 (RGE methodology reused for RGS) · Chapter 8 (SAFE-HAI / human oversight).

## Key findings

All claims **[verified from text]** unless tagged `[contested in chapter]`.

### ESG framing and regulation

- **ESG** screens investments on environment (E), social (S), and governance (G) performance; scores matter for AI applications and appear in regulation (EU AI Act acknowledges environmental benefits but allows supervisors to consider training energy for systemic-risk GPAI; UN AI Advisory Board interim report ties success to SDG achievement). [verified from text, L4146–4166]
- **Sustainable finance** aims to enhance returns while promoting social/environmental outcomes; long-term investment in sustainable activities. [verified from text, L4175–4180]
- ESG rating methodology is **not harmonized**; major platforms (Refinitiv, Bloomberg) produce scores that are hard to interpret; **greenwashing** misleads stakeholders. `[contested in chapter]` — authors cite refs 53–54 without reconciling provider disagreement in operational guidance. [verified from text, L4182–4191]
- E dimension: climate mitigation, pollutants, GHG; most popular ESG pillar. S: inequality, labor, human rights, employee safety. G: board conflicts, gender diversity. [verified from text, L4193–4213]

### Environmental sustainability — Bayesian ESG ensemble (Agosto et al. 2023)

- Case study: **1,382 European companies**; predict Bloomberg **credit rating** (investment grade vs speculative) from ESG scores with environmental focus. [verified from text, L4225–4331]
- Three provider scores: **MSCI** (0–10), **Refinitiv** (0–100), **S&P Global** (0–100 percentile); pairwise correlations modest (MSCI–S&P 0.372, MSCI–Refinitiv 0.383, Refinitiv–S&P 0.692). [verified from text, L4311–4366]
- **Bayesian ensemble** weights each provider score by in-sample predictive likelihood of rating partitions (quartile clusters); combined PD = PD(cluster j under P) × P(partition P). Authors claim model is sustainable, fair (averages providers), and explainable (weighted linear combination with interpretable posterior weights). `[contested in chapter]` — causal ESG→rating interpretation from correlational design. [verified from text, L4261–4300]
- Estimated weights: **MSCI 0.36, Refinitiv 0.37, S&P 0.27**; combined validation accuracy **~61%** vs 59%, 55%, 51% individually; ensemble stronger in extreme low-rating classes; order preserved when controlling for 13 financial ratios + market cap. [verified from text, L4368–4394]
- ESG score distributions left-skewed (many firms with weak ESG); rating distribution right-skewed (many high ratings) — both hinder predictive accuracy. [verified from text, L4343–4361]

### Social and governance sustainability — gender diversity (Bosone et al. 2022)

- **Female presence** (board %, female directors/executives, CEO, pay-gap indicators) treated as S and G proxy; five-year **default probability (PD)** as long-horizon financial sustainability outcome. [verified from text, L4404–4426]
- Sample: **>15,000 cross-sectional observations (2020)** from 12 European countries; North (8 countries, 329 firms) vs South (4 countries, 229 firms). [verified from text, L4481–4489]
- Gender indicators: equal-opportunity policies, health/safety policies, % women on board, female directors/executives, female CEO, gender pay gap breakout, fair remuneration; controls include market cap, ROA, ROE, ROIC, WACC, leverage, analyst rating. [verified from text, L4442–4479]
- Summary stats: Southern firms lower average 5y PD but higher ESG; more female executives/directors and higher board female % in South; Northern board compensation higher; fair remuneration policy rare (16/329 North, 7/228 South); gender pay gap reported in >half of Southern firms. [verified from text, L4491–4515]
- Authors warn top-level female representation alone is **partial** view; remuneration fairness and pay-gap reporting carry more signal given 37% EU women in managerial roles. [verified from text, L4517–4522]
- **Logistic regression:** country (North/South) positively correlated with PD; total ESG weak positive (10% sig); **S and G individually significant** (5% and 1%): +10% S → −0.5% PD, +10% G → −0.9% PD; total ESG +10% → +0.7% PD (offset); net E+S+G +10% each → ~−0.7% PD (~16% relative decrease vs ~4.3% average PD). [verified from text, L4538–4571]
- **% women on board** negatively correlated with PD; higher female board presence linked to better dialogue, decision quality, innovation. `[contested in chapter]` — correlational; mechanism claims from literature not experimentally isolated in chapter. [verified from text, L4573–4588]
- Conclusion: E, S, G indicators assess sustainability of firms that produce/deploy/use AI. [verified from text, L4590–4595]

### Model sustainability — RGS metric

- **Rank Graduation Sustainability (RGS)** = Σ(Q′ᵢ − QPᵢ) / Σ(Q′ᵢ − QFᵢ) where Q′ᵢ is dual Lorenz curve, QFᵢ full-model prediction ranks, QPᵢ ranks excluding E, S, or G (or all). Same properties as RGE; Python code deferred to Chapter 4. [verified from text, L4597–4617]
- **Table 7-1 scoring rubric:** Excellent 75–90%, Good 70–75%, Fair 60–70%, Borderline 40–60%, Poor 0–40%. [verified from text, L4619–4631]

### Mitigation

- **Social:** AI benefits healthcare but risks bias, hallucinations, privacy/human-rights harms, democracy threats, energy use; diverse datasets and development teams recommended. [verified from text, L4642–4658]
- **Economic:** Gen AI high capex vs uncertain ROI; some pilots shut off; AI should **augment** not replace workers; job-transition skills needed. `[contested in chapter]` — ROI claims anecdotal. [verified from text, L4660–4683]
- **Environmental:** AI can help environmental NGOs but datacenter/GPU energy for foundation models is high; mitigation via efficient hardware, greener algorithms, smaller models, alternative chip startups. [verified from text, L4685–4701]
