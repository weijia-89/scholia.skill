## scope

Chapter 1 is the **architectural framing chapter** for DDIA 2e. Within lines 106–1632 it establishes the book's trade-off lens and vocabulary before implementation chapters. Four major arcs:

1. **Operational vs analytical systems** — OLTP vs OLAP; business analysts vs data scientists; data warehouses, ETL, HTAP hybrids; data lakes; lakehouse; DataOps; systems of record vs derived data; unbundling databases.

2. **Cloud vs self-hosting** — build/buy spectrum; SaaS vs off-the-shelf vs bespoke; cloud economics; vendor lock-in; cloud-native separation of storage and compute; multitenant services; SRE vs customer ops.

3. **Distributed vs single-node** — reasons to distribute (geo, HA, scale reads, latency, elasticity, specialized hardware, compliance); downsides (latency, partial failure, debugging); microservices and serverless; cloud vs supercomputing/HPC.

4. **Law and society** — GDPR, EU AI Act, datensparsamkeit, compelled disclosure risks, SOC2/ISO27001 procurement pressure.

## key_findings

1. **Data-intensive definition** — Applications whose primary challenge is storing/processing large volumes, managing change, consistency under failure/concurrency, and availability—not raw compute parallelism. [verified, 143–149]

2. **Standard building blocks** — Databases, caches, search indexes, stream processing, batch processing; glue-code integration when single tools insufficient. [verified, 151–182]

3. **No universal best approach** — Technology choice depends on application needs; book teaches evaluation questions not dogma. [verified, 184–189]

4. **Stakeholder divergence** — Same dataset, different teams with implicit conflicting goals; architecture must surface trade-offs. [verified, 202–208]

5. **OLTP vs OLAP** — Interactive read/write/update (transaction processing) vs large scans/aggregations (analytics); fuzzy boundary; analysts use SQL/BI tools, not ad-hoc production DB access. [verified, 307–383]

6. **Data warehouse pattern** — Read-only ETL copy from operational systems; column-oriented layouts (Ch. 4); separation protects production and enables analyst freedom. [verified, 391–445]

7. **HTAP and hybrid** — SingleStore-style combined OLTP+OLAP possible but operational/analytical separation still common at scale (microservices → many OLTP DBs). [verified, 456–477]

8. **Data lake → lakehouse** — Raw files (Parquet etc.) for ML; warehouse can query lake; governance/privacy central to DataOps. [verified, 485–567]

9. **Systems of record vs derived** — Authoritative source vs redundant copies (indexes, caches, warehouses); derived data needs derivation pipelines; unbundling specialized DBs. [verified, 577–636]

10. **Cloud spectrum** — Bespoke → off-the-shelf self-hosted → managed cloud → SaaS; economics situational not universal. [verified, 641–699]

11. **Cloud pros/cons** — Elasticity, reduced ops burden vs lock-in, opaque failures, compliance limits, cost surprises at scale. [verified, 684–765]

12. **Cloud-native architecture** — IaaS vs PaaS/serverless layering; separation of storage and compute; object storage durability assumptions; multitenant cost efficiency. [verified, 776–904]

13. **Ops bifurcation** — Cloud provider SRE vs customer integration/observability; API hides machines; capacity planning → elasticity. [verified, 905–981]

14. **When to distribute** — Seven drivers listed; prefer single-node when possible (DuckDB, SQLite, KùzuDB for many workloads). [verified, 990–1106]

15. **Distributed downsides** — Network latency, partial failure, troubleshooting difficulty, consistency complexity. [verified, 1062–1103]

16. **Microservices/serverless** — Decomposition for team autonomy vs operational/API-evolution complexity; FaaS and managed data services as serverless data layer. [verified, 1108–1188]

17. **Privacy/regulation** — Legal constraints shape storage design; datensparsamkeit; risks of compelled access; no clear technical mapping yet. [verified, 1239–1336]
