  * Other abilities: empathy (understanding thinking, sensing feeling) + appropriate response

### 2.2 Trust

* Trust described as foundation of all organizational relationships
* Seven markers of trustworthy people: truth-telling, reliability, high-quality work, team orientation, sound decision-making, socially appropriate behavior, sensitivity to others' feelings
* Ethics framed as subset of trust — "principle-based standards or codes of behavior"
* Codes of conduct: examples given for confidentiality, fair treatment, truthful advertising, refusal of compromising gifts
* Key claim: "trust, once lost, is difficult to regain" \[stated as truism; see C-005 for revised confidence\]

### 2.3 Conversation and Listening

Seven conversation guidelines:

1. Assess purpose, audience, and context
2. Show interest (eye contact, nonverbal acknowledgment, paraphrasing)
3. Be respectful (validate; disagree tactfully; attend to intent not just words)
4. Be optimistic (avoid negative comments; use humor carefully)
5. Be appropriately assertive (not aggressive — confident self-advocacy)
6. Help keep conversation going (5W2H questions)
7. Ensure appropriate turn-taking

Five listening guidelines:

1. Really listen (no fake listening; be present physically and mentally)
2. Be responsive (verbal: "I see," "That's a good point"; nonverbal cues)
3. Orient body toward speaker (lean forward, eye contact, nod)
4. Pay attention to verbal and nonverbal messages (ears + eyes)
5. Test for understanding — reflective/active listening (paraphrase content and feelings)

### 2.4 Social Networking

* Value of networks = information, influence, help (including job search)
* Reciprocity expectation: "what you can give as well as what you can get"
* Six networking tactics: treat events as opportunities; develop conversation skills; be assertive in introductions; exchange business cards; circulate in large groups; maintain online presence (LinkedIn, Facebook)

### 2.5 Manners and Etiquette

Subcategories covered: General behavior, Meeting People, Dress and Grooming, Table Manners (American vs. Continental eating styles, seating protocol, dietary accommodations). US-centric norms with acknowledgment that standards vary by country.

## 3\. Management Effectiveness Claims

### 3.1 Management Basics

* Efficiency = minimizing resource use; Effectiveness = maximizing results
* Leadership vs. Management distinction: leaders = vision, future orientation, larger picture; managers = operations, processes, predictability, stability
* Formal power (positional authority, rewards, coercion) vs. Personal influence (knowledge + trust)
* Task orientation vs. Relationship orientation: effective managers balance both; too task-oriented → morale problems; too relationship-oriented → task failure

### 3.2 Four Management Functions

| Function | Communication Focus |
| --- | --- |
| Planning | Set goals; involve employees in decisions that affect them, require buy-in, leverage their knowledge, or develop them |
| Organizing | Clarify roles/responsibilities; delegate effectively; explain reporting; standardize without stifling creativity; don't micromanage |
| Monitoring | Communicate performance measures; hold regular reviews; give clear honest feedback; resolve violations without damaging relationships |
| Leading | Clarify mission; keep employees informed; build trust; create supportive environment; empower within guidelines; give frequent recognition |

Upward management guidance: clarify manager's expectations, keep manager informed, be honest but tactful, be efficient with manager's time, give appropriate compliments.

Ethical escalation: if manager acts unethically or in discriminatory manner, "you are not obligated to remain silent or comply." Document bad behavior privately. Consider alternatives from saying no to filing formal complaints.

### 3.3 Communication Barriers

Six barrier types:

1. Organizational — transmission error compounds with each relay; give critical messages directly
2. Physical — noise, distance, lighting, environmental interference
3. Technological — verify receipt of critical communications
4. Language and Cultural — non-native speakers don't comprehend like native speakers; translate materials; arrange interpreters; provide additional explanation
5. Physiological — hearing/visual impairments; unclear speech
6. Logical and Psychological — preoccupation, unique frames of reference ("meaning exists in the human mind, not in words themselves"), information manufacturing when gaps exist; emotions cause egocentrism, defensiveness, stress-driven illogical decisions; emotions are contagious (both negative and positive)

### 3.4 Managing a Team (Tuckman 1965)

Four stages: Forming → Storming → Norming → Performing

Forming: select for right knowledge/skills, strong interest/emotional commitment, right personal attributes (openness, cooperation, schedule discipline). Face-to-face initial meeting recommended even for virtual teams.

Storming: three conflict types:

* Content conflict — essential; focus on "what is right, not who is right"
* Procedure conflict — resolved by dialogue, not voting; "not my way versus your way, but our way"
* Relationship conflict — destructive; prevent through compatible selection; resolve through separation, interviewing, brainstorming solutions, evaluation, implementation, follow-up

Norming: safe atmosphere for disagreement; effective working procedures; acceptance of team personalities.

Performing: open/honest communication; diverse opinions accepted; self-motivation; team pride; minimal oversight needed.

Groupthink warning (Janis 1972): occurs when teams become too cohesive or dominated by strong leader. Members hesitate to raise opposing views. Prevent by encouraging all opinions, examining viable options, controlling dominant members.

### 3.5 Managing Diversity

* Workforce diversity: gender, race, age, religion, nationality, language, culture
* Hire and advance based on ability to produce work, not arbitrary factors
* Different approaches can achieve same results (example: relationship-oriented vs. task-oriented management)
* ADA (1990): prohibits discrimination based on disability; requires reasonable communication accommodations (braille, sign-language interpreters)
* International audiences: cultural differences, nonverbal communication differences, language differences

Language simplification guidelines for international audiences:

* Word level: simple concrete words; active voice; consistent terminology; avoid slang, puns, abbreviations, culturally symbolic language
* Sentence/paragraph level: simple construction; short paragraphs
* Visuals: internationally recognized icons; avoid culture-specific color symbolism
* Pilot test with target audience

### 3.6 Conducting Meetings

Three phases: Plan → Conduct → Follow up

Planning: clarify purpose; prepare agenda with appropriate sequencing and time allocation; arrange appropriate time/place/technology; notify participants in advance; prepare handouts/slideshows; arrange minute-taker; develop checklist.

Conducting — three parallel tracks:

| Track | Focus | Examples |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Task | Business content | Define problem, brainstorm, evaluate options, develop strategy, present research |
| Procedure | Process management | State agenda, assign speaking sequence, re-sequence, determine closing time |
| Relationship | People's feelings | Validate comments, paraphrase, draw out quiet people, check on feelings |

Seven facilitation responsibilities:

1. Be objective — suppress bias
2. Move discussion forward — discuss-decide-delegate pattern (who will do what by when)
3. Foster appropriate divergent and convergent thinking
4. Create psychologically safe environment (structured go-round technique)

Objection 2: The ingest assigns the Goleman EI-vs-IQ claim a confidence of 55, which is still too high given meta-analytic evidence shows EI predictive validity (r=.23) is less than half of GMA's established validity (r≈.50).

Objection 3: The ingest treats "meaning in mind, not words" as a borrowable concept from Baker without noting it's an unattributed restatement of Korzybski's general semantics, making the "borrowable from Baker" framing misleading.

Response: SUSTAINED in part. The concept originates with Korzybski (1933), not Baker. Baker presents it without attribution as folk wisdom about communication barriers. Skill incorporation row updated to note Korzybski origin. The concept's operational value for de-AI work is unchanged — the provenance correction is epistemic, not functional.
