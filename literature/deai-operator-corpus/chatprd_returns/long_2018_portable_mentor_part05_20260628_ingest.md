## 1) TL;DR (strongest claims first)

* Submission volume, not individual acceptance, is the correct unit of goal-setting: Long's "500 envelopes" strategy reframes rejection as progress. Mechanism is decoupling self-worth from editorial verdict. Directly borrowable for iterative revision workflows. \[speculative\] — practitioner testimony (N=1), no controlled comparison. \[c1=55, c2=48, c3=52; FINAL=52\]
* Finishing work is a discrete skill separate from generating it: Valian's observation that "attitudes toward finishing work most distinguish successful from unsuccessful workers" names a failure mode that maps onto deai-removal workflow stalls. \[verified\] — Valian 1977 full-text confirmed via Hunter College/Squarespace PDF. Note: Valian's essay is personal reflection + clinical observation, not empirical study. \[c1=58, c2=50, c3=55; FINAL=55\]
* Reading aloud to an audience is a revision technique, not a performance technique: mechanism is that oral delivery forces sound-level attention invisible on the page. \[speculative\] — widely attested practitioner consensus; no controlled study cited by Long or found in search.
* "Received diction" as a named deai signal class: Long's critique of default word choices ("home" for house, "a blonde") parallels RLHF-generic language patterns. \[inferred\] — Long does not mention AI or RLHF; the deai mapping is this ingest's inference from Long's diction critique to the deai framework. The inference chain: received diction = writer defaulting to expected words → RLHF models also default to expected words → same audit technique applies.
* The ceramics parable (quantity drives quality) is an adapted retelling, not empirical evidence: the real origin is photographer Jerry Uelsmann's classroom gambit at University of Florida. Bayles and Orland changed medium from photography to ceramics with acknowledged "literary license." \[verified\] — Orland 2016 email via Austin Kleon confirmed provenance. The moral may hold but the specific story is a teaching fable. Simonton's equal-odds rule provides independent empirical backing for the quantity-quality relationship. \[verified\] — Simonton historiometric work confirmed via APA PsycNet.

### What I could not verify

* Long's personal submission statistics (500 envelopes, 70 rejections before first acceptance, 300 envelopes yielding 11 acceptances, 8000 copies sold of first edition) — these are self-reported figures with no external corroboration available. Treated as practitioner testimony, not verifiable data.
* Whether Valian's observation about finishing distinguishing successful from unsuccessful workers has been empirically tested since 1977. The Valian essay is confirmed real but is reflective, not experimental.
* Whether the "imminent exposure" effect (CM-01) has been studied in writing revision contexts specifically. General accountability research exists but was not searched exhaustively.
* Whether read-aloud revision (CM-07) has been studied in controlled conditions comparing silent vs. oral revision outcomes.

### What additional evidence would change the assessment

* A controlled study comparing revision quality under "external-eye" conditions vs. private conditions would promote CM-01 from \[speculative\] to \[inferred\] or \[verified\].
* Replication or challenge of Simonton's equal-odds rule in literary (vs. scientific) creative production would sharpen CM-08.

* A study correlating writer embarrassment with independently assessed quality would either confirm or kill CM-10.
