## 2\. Chapter Summary (ch02 slice)

The slice assembles three classical source texts under the frame "What Is Rhetoric?" and bridges them to professional writing:

* Aristotle Rhetoric I.2–I.3: defines rhetoric as dynamis (capacity) to see available means of persuasion in each case; classifies pisteis into atechnic/entechnic and the three artistic proofs (ethos, pathos, logos); introduces enthymeme and paradigm as rhetorical syllogism and induction; distinguishes common topoi from idia; names three species of rhetoric (deliberative, judicial, epideictic).
* Aristotle Nicomachean Ethics VI.3–5: draws the epistemic triad — science (necessary, eternal, demonstrable), art (reasoned capacity to make, concerned with the variable), practical wisdom (reasoned capacity to act regarding human goods, also variable). Rhetoric occupies the art slot but bleeds into practical wisdom.
* Rhetorica Ad Herennium I.1–4 (attr. Cicero/Caplan): defines speaker's task as treating matters fixed by law and custom for citizenship; names five canons (invention, arrangement, style, memory, delivery); names six parts of discourse (introduction, statement of facts, division, proof, refutation, conclusion); learning triad: theory, imitation, practice.
* Peeples Ch 2 intro: frames the professional-writing-and-rhetoric relationship as either two adjacent fields or one interdisciplinary field; poses five guiding questions on social situation, practice vs. productive art, writer as rhetor/author, organizational authorship.
* Faigley "Nonacademic Writing: The Social Perspective" (partial): textual, individual, and social perspectives on writing research; discourse community as analytic frame.

## 4\. Key Conceptual Architecture

### 4a. Aristotle's definition as dynamis, not product

C-001 \[verified\]: Rhetoric is classified as dynamis — a capacity or potentiality. Kennedy note 34: "He identifies the genus to which rhetoric belongs as dynamis: 'ability, capacity, faculty.'"

C-002 \[verified\]: The actuality rhetoric produces is not a text or persuasion itself but the act of seeing how persuasion may be effected. Kennedy note 34: "The actuality produced by the potentiality of rhetoric is not the written or oral text of a speech, or even persuasion but the art of 'seeing' how persuasion may be effected."

Craft-lane relevance \[tic_enrichment\]: The dynamis framing resists product-fixation. For voice work, this means the skill is pattern-recognition for persuasive affordances in a situation, not adherence to templates.

### 4b. Three artistic pisteis as analytical frame

| Pistis | Mechanism | Aristotle's anchor | Craft implication |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Ethos | Character revealed in the speech itself, not prior reputation | I.2.4 — "should result from speech, not from a previous opinion" \[C-003 verified\] | Voice is constructed in-text. Authority is earned per-document, not imported by credential. \[tic_enrichment\] |
| Pathos | Disposing the hearer's emotional state via speech | I.2.5 — judgment differs when grieved vs. rejoicing \[C-006 verified via Q-006\] | Emotional register is a persuasive instrument, not decoration. De-AI work must preserve intentional affect moves. \[both\] |
| Logos | Showing truth or apparent truth through argument | I.2.6 \[C-007a verified via Q-007\] | Logical structure carries independent persuasive weight. Enthymematic compression (omitting known premises) is a feature, not a deficiency. |

Note on ethos-pathos-logos shorthand \[C-004 verified\]: Kennedy note 40 states this shorthand "does not represent Aristotle's own usage." For Aristotle, ethos and pathos are attributes of persons, not of speeches. The ingest uses the shorthand as a convenience, per Kennedy's allowance.

### 4c. Enthymeme and paradigm as the two engines

C-005 \[verified\]: Enthymeme omits known premises — Rhetoric I.2.13: "if one of these is known, it does not have to be stated, since the hearer supplies it."

C-006a \[verified\]: Kennedy note 60 confirms rhetoric "addressed to an audience that cannot be assumed to follow intricate logical argument or will be impatient with premises that seem unnecessary steps in the argument."

* Enthymeme: rhetorical syllogism. Draws from premises "true for the most part" (probabilities) and signs. Omits premises the audience already holds.
* Paradigm: rhetorical induction. Moves from particular to particular via unstated universal. The Dionysius/bodyguard example (I.2.19).

Craft note \[tic_enrichment\]: Enthymematic structure — conclusion + reason, universal suppressed — is the natural shape of professional prose. Explicit syllogistic form reads as over-explained.

C-007 \[speculative\]: The claim that "RLHF-trained text often over-specifies premises (hedging, qualifying) where enthymematic compression would be stronger" has no textual warrant in this slice. It is an inference from combining Aristotelian theory with knowledge of LLM behavior. Failure mode: RLHF outputs may over-specify for reasons unrelated to enthymematic logic (e.g., liability avoidance, instruction-following). The Aristotelian frame does not distinguish these causes.

### 4d. Topoi: common vs. specific

* Common topoi (koina): strategies of argument applicable across domains — e.g., "the more and the less" (I.2.21). Formal moves, content-agnostic.
* Specific topoi (idia): premises drawn from particular disciplines — physics, ethics, politics. The more a speaker relies on idia, the more the argument becomes that discipline's argument and less "purely rhetorical" (I.2.21–22).

Craft note \[tic_enrichment\]: Professional writers navigate the common/specific boundary. Generic rhetorical strategies (framing, contrast, amplification) combine with domain-specific knowledge. Voice work should respect domain-specific register without collapsing into jargon or into generic "clear writing" advice.

### 4e. Three species of rhetoric

| Species | Time orientation | Telos (end) | Audience role |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Deliberative (symbouleutikon) | Future | Advantageous / harmful | Judge of future happenings |
| Judicial (dikanikon) | Past | Just / unjust | Judge of past events |
| Epideictic (epideiktikon) | Present (primarily) | Honorable / shameful | Spectator / observer |

C-012 \[speculative\]: The claim that "most business writing is deliberative, documentation is judicial, marketing is epideictic" is an analytic inference. No source in this slice supports it. Failure mode: Modern documents routinely blend species (a product launch document praises, recommends, and justifies simultaneously). The mapping is a heuristic, not a classification.

### 4f. The epistemic triad from NE VI

| Category | Object | Relation to necessity | Capacity type |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Science (episteme) | Necessary, eternal things | Invariable | Demonstrative knowledge |
| Art (techne) | Things capable of being otherwise | Variable | Reasoned capacity to make |
| Practical wisdom (phronesis) | Human goods | Variable | Reasoned capacity to act |

Rhetoric as techne: "a state concerned with making, involving a true course of reasoning" (NE VI.4 \[Q-012 verified\]).

C-011 \[inferred — with misattribution corrected\]: The Peeples intro says "though Aristotle seems to argue that rhetoric is a productive art, his works, as well as others within the rhetorical tradition, seem to suggest that rhetoric is also a form of practice." Peeples does not use the word phronesis. The ingest's mapping of practice to phronesis is a defensible inference from the NE VI triad, but should not be attributed to Peeples.

C-013 \[speculative\]: The claim that "the techne/phronesis split maps directly onto deai work — removal as techne, voice priming as phronesis" is an analytic inference with no textual warrant. Failure mode: Both deai_removal and tic_enrichment involve contextual judgment; the clean split may obscure that removal decisions also require phronesis (e.g., deciding whether a specific hedge is RLHF residue or intentional).

### 4g. Five canons (Ad Herennium)

Invention, arrangement, style, memory, delivery — with learning via theory, imitation, practice. The Ad Herennium is more prescriptive/procedural than Aristotle. It specifies six parts of discourse under invention (introduction, statement of facts, division, proof, refutation, conclusion).

C-014 \[inferred — scope narrowed\]: Ad Herennium I.3: "Imitation stimulates us to attain, in accordance with a studied method, the effectiveness of certain models in speaking." This is compatible with structural borrowing, but the text does not distinguish structural borrowing from surface-level mimicry. The "not pastiche" constraint is the ingest's inference, not the Ad Herennium's instruction. The warrant supports borrowing-via-imitation in general; the anti-pastiche constraint must come from elsewhere (parent canon).
