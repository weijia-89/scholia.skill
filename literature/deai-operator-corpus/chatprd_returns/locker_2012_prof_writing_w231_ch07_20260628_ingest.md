## 2\. Chapter Summary

Ch07 teaches how to organize and deliver messages the audience does not want to receive. Core thesis: negative messages must balance clarity (audience must understand the refusal) with goodwill (audience should feel treated fairly). The chapter provides three distinct organizational patterns keyed to audience relationship (clients/customers, superiors, peers/subordinates), then details the component parts: subject lines, buffers, reasons, refusals, alternatives, and endings. Secondary topics include apology strategy, tone management, psychological reactance, and recasting negatives as positive or persuasive messages.

## 5\. Key Concepts Extracted

### 5a. Three Organizational Patterns (Figure 10.1)

Pattern A — Clients/Customers:

1. Reason before refusal (prepares audience)
2. Negative information stated once, clearly
3. Alternative or compromise
4. Positive, forward-looking ending

Pattern B — Superiors:

1. Describe the problem (clear, unemotional)
2. Explain how it happened (background factors)
3. Describe options for fixing it (with advantages/disadvantages)
4. Recommend a solution, ask for action

Pattern C — Peers/Subordinates:

1. Describe the problem (clear, unemotional)
2. Present alternative or compromise
3. Ask for input or action

Confidence: 40 \[speculative\] — Prescriptive pedagogy, not empirically validated framework. Useful as scaffolding.

### 5b. Parts of a Negative Message

* Subject lines: topic without specific negative; use negative subject line when reader might ignore routine-looking message
* Buffers: neutral/positive opener to delay negative; types include good news, facts/chronology, enclosure references, thanks, statements of principle; use only when audience culturally values harmony or buffer serves additional purpose. Locker's own 1999 research found buffers did not improve reader response (self-citation, not attributed in textbook).
* Reasons: must be real, not proxy; strong and watertight only; omit rather than use weak reason; never hide behind "company policy"; never say "cannot" when you mean "choose not to"
* Refusals: embed in same paragraph as reason; implied refusal preferred if crystal clear; goal is to close the door
* Alternatives: restore psychological freedom (Brehm & Brehm 1981 reactance theory, application to written business messages is \[inferred\])
* Endings: reference the alternative; look forward; avoid insincere closings

### 5c. Apology Framework

* Apologize only when at fault
* Early, brief, sincere — once only
* Do not dwell on what went wrong; focus on corrective action
* "I'm sorry" implies blame acceptance — avoid when not at fault \[inferred, no citation provided\]
* When not at fault, provide explanation instead of apology
* Pair apology with concrete benefit when possible (Hallmark, Apple iPhone, airline examples from source text)
* Hospital disclosure + apology reduces litigation: general pattern confirmed across institutions \[verified\]; specific 40%/23% figures are \[ZOMBIE — unverifiable to primary methodology\]

### 5d. Tone Management

* Check for positive emphasis and you-attitude at word and idea level
* Avoid: "I am afraid that," "I am sorry that we are unable," "I am sure you will agree that," "Unfortunately"
* Physical appearance and timing convey tone

### 5e. Recasting Strategies

* Recast as positive: when negative directly leads to benefit audience wants
* Recast as persuasive: frame problem as request for audience help
* Southwest Airlines example from source text

### 5f. Psychological Reactance (Brehm & Brehm 1981)

* Negative messages limit freedom; audience may assert freedom destructively
* Application to written business communication is Locker's \[inferred\] extension, not empirically demonstrated for that medium
* Alternative offers constructive channel for reactance \[inferred\]
