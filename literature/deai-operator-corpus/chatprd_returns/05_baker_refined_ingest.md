## Key Findings / Claims Register

### C-001: Communication as primary management tool \[verified\]

The text asserts communication is "the most vital skill of managers" and that all management functions (planning, organizing, monitoring, leading) depend on it.

Source anchor: "Communication is the means by which managers manage." (Ch 1, p. 1)

Confidence: High for the claim that communication skill correlates with management effectiveness. This is a foundational premise of business communication textbooks, supported by NACE survey data and management literature \[c1=85, c2=80, c3=82\]. FINAL=82.

### C-002: Gardner's four social effectiveness factors as organizing framework \[verified\]

Baker et al. adopt Gardner (1993) via Goleman (1995): self-awareness, self-management, social awareness, relationship management. Presented as a 2x2 matrix (self/other x awareness/management).

Source anchor: "Gardner (1993) suggests four abilities to be socially effective" (Ch 1, p. 2)

Note: The text cites Gardner 1993 and Goleman 1995 but does not distinguish between Gardner's multiple intelligences framework and Goleman's emotional intelligence model. The 2x2 matrix presented is closer to Goleman/Boyatzis EI competency model than to Gardner's original MI theory. \[inferred: textbook conflates two related but distinct frameworks\]

### C-003: Trust as foundation of professional relationships \[verified\]

Seven behavioral markers of trust listed: truth-telling, reliability, high-quality work, team orientation, sound decision-making, social appropriateness, sensitivity to others' feelings.

Source anchor: "Everything that happens within and between organizations is based on some type of relationship, and the more the participants can be trusted, the better the relationship will be." (Ch 1, p. 5)

### C-004: Six communication barriers taxonomy \[verified\]

Organizational, physical, technological, language/cultural, physiological, logical/psychological. The logical/psychological category introduces preoccupation, frame of reference, and information manufacturing as sub-barriers.

Source anchor: "Be aware of six types of barriers to communication." (Ch 1, p. 15)

### C-005: Tuckman's team development stages \[verified\]

Forming-storming-norming-performing model (Tuckman 1965) applied to workplace team management. Text provides actionable guidance per stage.

Source anchor: "Teams typically navigate through four stages of development: forming, storming, norming, and performing (Tuckman 1965)" (Ch 1, p. 17)

### C-006: Three conflict types in teams \[verified\]

Content conflict (essential/productive), procedure conflict (process decisions), relationship conflict (destructive). Text argues content conflict is healthy; relationship conflict requires intervention.

Source anchor: "Three general categories of conflict can emerge." (Ch 1, p. 19)

### C-007: Groupthink as team pathology \[verified\]

Janis (1972) cited. Text identifies three causes: reluctance to appear unsupportive, fear of negative peer pressure, excessive trust in leader.

Source anchor: "a phenomenon called groupthink (Janis 1972)" (Ch 1, p. 20)

### C-008: Meaning exists in minds, not in words \[verified\]

"meaning exists in the human mind, not in words themselves" — a constructivist communication principle embedded in the barriers discussion.

Source anchor: "when you use a specific word like 'meeting,' ten people will think of the word in ten different ways—showing that meaning exists in the human mind, not in words themselves." (Ch 1, p. 16)

### C-009: Language simplification for non-native audiences — prescriptive \[verified\]

Text instructs: "simplify your writing and explanations" for audiences whose primary language differs. Recommends simple concrete words, active voice, strong subjects, vivid verbs, simple verb tenses.

Source anchor: "use simple, concrete words; avoid abstract words and lengthy compound words. Use the active voice with strong subjects, vivid verbs, and simple verb tenses." (Ch 1, p. 23)

Conflict flag: This prescriptive move assumes a native-speaker author simplifying for non-native readers. For an ESL operator, adopting this as a self-directed rule risks flattening legitimate L1-influenced syntax into "simple English." See Kill-list K-002.

### C-010: Emotions are contagious in organizations \[verified\]

"one person's bad attitude can cause others to have a bad attitude. However, the reverse is also true"

Source anchor: Ch 1, p. 16

Note: Consistent with Hatfield et al. (1994) emotional contagion research. \[inferred: textbook does not cite this literature directly\]

## Lane B — tic_enrichment

### Borrowable structures for tic.skill

1. Hook-ending pattern (CM-001): Transferable to written communication — end paragraphs or sections with a question or forward-pointing statement that pulls the reader onward. Voice-neutral, ESL-safe.

2. Content vs. feeling paraphrase split (CM-002): Applicable when writing stakeholder summaries or meeting notes. Separating factual content from emotional subtext is a structural move that strengthens analytical writing regardless of L1.

3. Task-relationship axis (CM-003): Useful as a self-check lens: "Is this draft too task-heavy (all demands, no rapport)? Too relationship-heavy (all softening, no actionable content)?" Operator can calibrate without importing Baker's specific register.

4. Meaning-in-minds (CM-005): Reinforces the tic.skill principle that the operator's job is to be understood, not to sound native. Multiple valid encodings of the same meaning are legitimate.

5. Information manufacturing (C-004 sub-claim): When writers leave gaps, readers fill them with assumptions. Operational: be explicit about reasoning, especially in cross-cultural or async contexts. Don't assume shared defaults.

### What Baker Ch 1 does NOT provide for tic_enrichment

* No sentence-level craft moves (those appear in Ch 3–5 per TOC, not in slice)
* No genre-specific templates (Ch 3 OABC pattern, Ch 8 proposals, Ch 10 reports — all deferred)
* No revision heuristics (Ch 5 — deferred)
* No visual design principles (Ch 4, 11 — deferred)

Recommendation: Ch 1 yields conceptual frameworks, not sentence-craft. The high-value tic_enrichment chapters are likely Ch 3 (OABC + message categories), Ch 5 (revision process), and Ch 12 (presentation structure). Prioritize those slices if additional ingests are planned.

## Kill-list / Do-Not-Borrow

### K-001: Personality prescriptivism (Table 1.2 Personal Attributes Chart)

The text presents a self-assessment with normative ratings: "Extroverted rather than introverted" scored as if extroversion is categorically better. "Calm and composed rather than anxious and nervous" similarly framed.

Do not borrow: This is personality-norming, not communication craft. An introverted operator's writing voice is not a deficiency. Importing this framing would contradict both ESL-preserve and DEAI principles.

### K-002: "Simplify for non-native speakers" as self-directed rule

C-009 instructs writers to simplify vocabulary and syntax for international audiences. As written, it assumes a native-English author accommodating non-native readers. For an L1-Chinese operator, adopting this as a self-directed writing rule risks:

* Flattening legitimate complex syntax into "simple English"
* Treating the operator's natural register as the problem rather than the audience's needs
* Reinforcing RLHF-adjacent "sound more native" pressure

Do not borrow as self-directed rule. May borrow selectively when the operator IS writing for a known low-English-proficiency audience, but the move must be audience-driven, not self-policing. \[esl_preserve\]

### K-003: Etiquette prescriptivism (manners section)

The table manners, dress code, and social protocol sections (pp. 10–12) are culturally specific to US professional norms. Borrowing these as "professional communication" rules would import cultural conformity pressure irrelevant to writing craft.

Do not borrow: No writing-craft value. Cultural information only.

### K-004: "Avoid excessive jewelry or faddish hair and dress styles that draw attention to you"

Appearance-policing with conformity bias. Irrelevant to communication skill. Kill.

### K-005: Gender-essentialist management framing

"a woman might be more relationship oriented and a man more task oriented in the way they manage a team" (Ch 1, p. 20). Presented as neutral observation but reinforces gender stereotypes.

Do not borrow: Stereotyping, not craft. \[deai_removal\]

## Second-pass: Steelman and Falsifiers

### Steelman: "Break one Baker rule for voice"

Baker's strongest prescriptive rule in this slice is the task-relationship balance (CM-003). A steelman case for breaking it: some professional contexts reward deliberate imbalance. A crisis communication should be almost entirely task-oriented. A condolence note should be almost entirely relationship-oriented. The "balance" prescription is a heuristic, not a law. Operator should treat it as a starting point and deliberately break it when context demands.

### Falsifiers per craft move promoted

| Craft Move | Falsifier |
| --- | --- |
| CM-001 Hook-ending | Can become formulaic if every turn ends with a question. Over-use signals insecurity or faux-interest. Use selectively. |
| CM-002 Reflective listening | In written communication, excessive paraphrasing ("So what you're saying is...") reads as condescending. Appropriate for live conversation; adapt for writing by showing understanding through action, not restatement. |
| CM-003 Task-relationship balance | See steelman above. Context-dependent, not universal. |
| CM-005 Meaning-in-minds | Can become an excuse for imprecision. "People will interpret differently anyway" does not justify vague writing. The principle supports audience analysis, not laziness. |
