## Key Concepts Extracted

1. Archaeological method as organizing metaphor

  * Lateral assembly (regularities, rules), not deep excavation (intentionality)
  * Shards → discursive formations → subject positions
  * Henry's 7-year dataset as multi-site dig
  * Lane: both — "assemble shards" resists master-narrative impulse, maps to RLHF's tendency toward false coherence

2. Narratological framework for reading workplace writers

  * Chatman model: Real Author → Implied Author → (Narrator) → (Narratee) → Implied Reader → Real Reader
  * Applied to nonfiction workplace texts: separates biological writer from textual self-representation
  * Implied author = responsible for "values and cultural norms" the text adheres to
  * Lane: tic_enrichment — narrator/implied-author split diagnoses RLHF-voice; when an AI text's implied author is blandly helpful and authority-free, the Taylorization is visible

3. Taylorization-composition parallel

  * Current-traditional rhetoric (Harvard model) → mastery of forms, no disciplinary content
  * Taylorization of factories → standardized movements → standardized writing
  * Both strip first-person authority; both serve "social efficiency" models
  * Sarmiento (via Hull): 95% of U.S. companies still organize along Taylorist principles \[single source, low confidence\]
  * Lane: deai_removal — RLHF training is a third-generation Taylorization of discourse

4. High-volume → high-value economy shift

  * Reich: "profits derive not from scale and volume but from continuous discovery of new linkages between solutions and needs"
  * Zuboff: "informated" workplace → "learning is the new form of labor"
  * Emig (1977): writing as mode of learning, not neutral medium
  * Lane: tic_enrichment — writer-as-learner justifies voice distinctiveness as organizational value

5. Author-function as exclusion mechanism

  * Foucault → Chartier → Crowley: "author" = published literary figure
  * Professional writers self-exclude from "writer" identity (Ann)
  * Crowley's writing/Writing split: instrumental writing can be done "by a machine"
  * "Each such effort is expendable" — deficit model renders all non-literary composition as "not yet real"
  * Lane: both — RLHF reproduces this exclusion; deai restores authorship

6. Machine-integration resistance (Johnson-Eilola/Lunsford)

  * Johnson-Eilola: Taylorism "always falls short" because people resist integration into the machine
  * Computer's omnipresence requires investigating subjectivities produced through machine-mediated composition
  * Lunsford: corporate appropriation of authorship — legal teams answer "What Is an Author?" with "us"
  * Writers need "alternative forms of subjectivity and alternative modes of ownership"
  * Lane: both — this is the deai argument stated in 2000 vocabulary; machine-mediated composition is now AI-mediated composition

7. Writer self-conceptualization inventory (Chapter 2 empirical core)

  * Writer as medium: "flowing out of me naturally" (Leigh), "stories demanding to be told" (Lois)
  * Writing = literary authorship: Branden "equated writing with Shakespeare"; Mandy "not a writer yet" because unpublished
  * Writing as world-encounter: exploration, learning, observation, interviewing
  * Writing exposes oneself: vulnerability, scrutiny, assessment by others
  * Writing linked to feelings: refuge, therapy, catharsis, frustration, passion
  * Personal/professional binary: strongly recurrent; writers are "bivalent" between personal and professional selves
  * Professional writing as collective: editing others, managing projects, multitasking, diplomacy, deadline-meeting
  * Lane: tic_enrichment — these conceptualizations reveal the emotional and cognitive stakes that RLHF-output lacks entirely; an AI text has no vulnerability, no exposure, no bivalence

8. Discursive self as multiple

  * Not unified expressivist self but Foucauldian subject positions
  * Classroom transcript (Anne, Nidean, Branden) demonstrates: single speaker instantiates student-narrator, writer-narrator, community-representative, peer-collaborator within five minutes
  * \[esl_preserve\]: L1-influenced syntax is one more "social force entering discourse through the I" — not deficiency but subject position

## Adversarial Self-Review

1. Coverage gap: Part Two body (Chapters 3–5) is not in the attachment. This is where Henry's empirical workplace-writing inventories live — organizational features, discursive features, bulleted lists of specific structures and practices. The current ingest covers theoretical framing (Introduction, Ch 1) and writer profiles (Ch 2), not the fieldwork data. A second ingest from Chapters 3–5 would substantially increase actionable craft moves.

2. Temporal limits: Publication date 2000. All workplace observations predate digital collaboration tools. The Taylorization parallel holds structurally but specific workplace-writing practices described are dated. Johnson-Eilola's "machine" metaphor is productively ambiguous — it was about computers in 2000, but maps to AI now. This mapping is an interpretive move, not something Henry himself claims.

3. The 95% Taylorism claim (Sarmiento via Hull) is a single-source assertion with no methodology disclosed. Henry presents it as a fact. I've capped confidence at 45.

4. Writer-as-medium narrative: Henry's researchers describe writing as "flowing out of me naturally" and "stories demanding to be told." This is the same phenomenology that RLHF-trained models simulate — fluent, frictionless output. The deai insight is that real human writing is protean (hard/easy/humdrum), emotionally charged, and vulnerability-producing. RLHF output is none of these things, which is precisely the signal.

5. The Foucault/archaeology metaphor risks becoming unfalsifiable. Henry doesn't address this. Operator should use the framework selectively.
