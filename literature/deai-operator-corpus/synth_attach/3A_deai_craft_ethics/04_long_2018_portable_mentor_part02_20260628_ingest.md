## Synthesis — Core Craft Moves Extracted (revised, post-adversarial)

6 distinct moves after consolidating overlaps.

### Move 1: Structure Extraction Protocol \[lane: tic_enrichment\]

Long's central pedagogy: read a model piece literally, extract its structural skeleton, then write your own material into that skeleton. The protocol:

* Count physical divisions (sections, white space, numbers, subtitles)
* Count paragraphs — treat each as a container with one controlling idea
* Map scenes (setting shifts = new scene)
* Note structural shifts: concrete narration vs. philosophy/metaphor
* Compare beginning to end for mirroring
* Track person shifts (I → you → he → we) and tense shifts

Paragraph count, not word count, is the primary structural metric. A fixed paragraph count (e.g., 13) serves as a constraint — forces compression, prevents slight pieces, and makes a daunting subject manageable. Break up ballooning paragraphs only at the end.

Confidence: 55 \[expert opinion with worked demonstrations on two models; no outcome data\]

Scope restriction: This protocol is demonstrated on literary essays. Applicability to technical, procedural, or research documents is \[speculative\] and requires operator testing.

Borrowing guardrails (from Long, aligning with canon):

* Choose a subject different from the model
* Work on material of central importance to you — this acts as counterweight to the model's influence
* Use a variety of models to avoid becoming a weak imitator
* Final step: discard the model, shape the piece on its own terms
* Verify you have not unconsciously taken word, image, or idea

### Move 2: Discovery Writing ↔ Structural Writing Cycle \[lane: both\]

Long rejects the binary of generative free-writing vs. planned structure. The cycle:

1. Write in notebook to find out what you have to say (discovery)
2. Deliberate on structure
3. Write again into the structure
4. Return to notebook to explore further as needed
5. Discard the model; shape your own piece on its own terms

The warning: skipping step 1 produces "stuffing a sausage" (Q-002). Never reaching step 2 is "the road to Never Never Land — never finishing, never publishing."

"Discovery writing" attributed to Jack Remick and Robert J. Ray \[verified as Long's attribution; primary verification would require their texts\].

Confidence: 50 \[expert opinion, pedagogically coherent, no empirical validation\]

### Move 3: Theme Structure — 13-Paragraph Template \[lane: tic_enrichment\]

Long's anatomy of Sadoff's "Ben Webster" (13 paragraphs, 1,763 words):

| Para | Function |
| --- | --- |
| 1 | Physical portrait + time/place + writer's connection to subject |
| 2–3 | Subject in action + context of their world |
| 4–5 | Generalizing statements about the subject's domain |
| 6–7 | Personal response ("I") + characterization of the work |
| 8 | Universal "we" statement about society |
| 9 | Biography in present tense |
| 10 | Career arc — rise, fall, resurrection, demise |
| 11 | Elegy — the late work |
| 12 | Lessons as a creator |
| 13 | Larger meanings for us |

Note: Olson's "Home" uses 20 paragraphs for the same structure type — the 13-paragraph count is a starting harness, not a law.

Confidence: 50 \[demonstrated on one model; one planned application (Agnes Denes) not confirmed as completed\]

### Move 4: Collage Structure — Thread-Weaving Without Connective Tissue \[lane: tic_enrichment\]

Long's analysis of Biss's "Babylon" (47 sections, 2,123 words):

* Sections are short — 12 of 47 are one or two lines
* Separated by white space (no transitions)
* Coherence comes from repeated location-threads (Babylon, California, New York) and theme-threads (plants/gardens, exile/migration, wounding/claiming)
* Frame: begins and ends with hanging gardens of Babylon
* No connective tissue between leaps in time/place — juxtaposition itself creates meaning

Variants: abecedarian (26 sections, A–Z), numbered collage (number should bear on subject).

Confidence: 52 \[one complete demonstration (Biss), one truncated (Madden)\]

### Move 5: The Universal "We" as Craft Skill \[lane: tic_enrichment\] \[esl_preserve\]

Long coaches writers to practice speaking in the universal "we" — to "claim authority" without hedging (Q-005). She acknowledges risk (clichéd, overgeneralized, arrogant) and prescribes accuracy and nonjudgmental tone as correctives.

Operator relevance: \[esl_preserve\] — the decision to deploy "we" claims is a rhetorical choice, not a native-speaker marker \[inferred: Long frames it as a learnable skill → skills are not native-speaker markers\]. The risk in the operator's context: ungrounded "we" claims can read as RLHF-adjacent generalizing. Deploy only when the claim is specific and defensible.

Confidence: 50 \[expert opinion; the skill is clearly described but the quality criteria — "accuracy," "nonjudgmental tone" — are vague\]

### Move 6: Content Suggests Form \[lane: tic_enrichment\]

Long's examples of organic form-finding:

* "Archaeology of Childhood" — shaped as academic archaeology article because content was about returning to ruins
* Francine du Plessix Gray's "The Work of Mourning" — five parts mapping Kübler-Ross's five stages of grief
* "Genome Tome" — 23 chapters for 23 chromosome pairs
* Nonliterary texts as structures: questionnaires, contributor's notes, epistolary forms

The principle: form exists to intensify meaning. "The content can suggest its own form."

Confidence: 50 \[expert opinion; examples are compelling but the principle is aesthetic, not falsifiable\]
