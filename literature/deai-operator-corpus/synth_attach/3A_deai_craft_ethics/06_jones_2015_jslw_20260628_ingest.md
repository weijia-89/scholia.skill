## 5\. Metric Glossary (L2SCA 14 Measures)

IMPORTANT: Two classification schemas exist. Lu (2010) Table 1 uses five "types." Lu & Ai (2015) uses five "dimensions" with different names (SQ-05, SQ-06). The 2015 paper says Table 1 is "adapted from Lu (2010)," meaning the measure-to-dimension assignments may have been reorganized. Without access to the 2015 Table 1, the exact 2015 assignments are unknown for some measures.

Lu (2010) classification (verified — SQ-11, from full text at jbe-platform.com):

Type 1 — Length of production unit:

* MLS: Mean Length of Sentence (words/sentence)
* MLT: Mean Length of T-unit (words/T-unit)
* MLC: Mean Length of Clause (words/clause)

Type 2 — Sentence complexity:

* C/S: Clauses per Sentence

Type 3 — Subordination:

* C/T: Clauses per T-unit
* CT/T: Complex T-unit Ratio
* DC/C: Dependent Clauses per Clause
* DC/T: Dependent Clauses per T-unit

Type 4 — Coordination:

* CP/C: Coordinate Phrases per Clause
* CP/T: Coordinate Phrases per T-unit
* T/S: T-units per Sentence (sentence coordination ratio)

Type 5 — Particular structures:

* CN/C: Complex Nominals per Clause
* CN/T: Complex Nominals per T-unit
* VP/T: Verb Phrases per T-unit

Lu & Ai (2015) five dimensions (from introduction, SQ-05): length of production unit, amount of subordination, amount of coordination, degree of phrasal sophistication, overall sentence complexity.

Mapping discrepancy: Lu (2010) calls Type 2 "Sentence complexity" and Type 5 "Particular structures." Lu & Ai (2015) uses "overall sentence complexity" and "phrasal sophistication" as dimension names. It is plausible that the 2015 paper mapped CN/C, CN/T to "phrasal sophistication" and C/S (plus perhaps T/S) to "overall sentence complexity," but this is \[speculative\] without the 2015 Table 1. VP/T placement in the 2015 schema is \[unknown\].

CORRECTION FROM PRIOR VERSION: The prior draft placed T/S under "Sentence complexity" and VP/T under "Phrasal sophistication." Both assignments were WRONG per Lu (2010). T/S is Type 4 (Coordination). VP/T is Type 5 (Particular structures). The 2015 assignments remain unconfirmed.

Key definitions (verified — SQ-12, SQ-13, SQ-14, from Lu 2010 full text):

* T-unit: "one main clause plus any subordinate clause or nonclausal structure that is attached to or embedded in it" (Hunt, 1970, cited in Lu 2010)
* Complex T-unit: a T-unit that contains a dependent clause (Casanave, 1994, cited in Lu 2010)
* Complex nominal: (i) nouns plus adjective, possessive, prepositional phrase, relative clause, participle, or appositive; (ii) nominal clauses; (iii) gerunds and infinitives in subject position (Cooper, 1976, cited in Lu 2010)

These metrics measure structural features. Higher subordination or phrasal complexity is NOT inherently "better" writing. \[esl_preserve\]

### Three Strongest Hostile Expert Objections

Objection 1: "The 3-of-14 finding for aggregated NNS is exactly what you'd expect from pooling heterogeneous groups — it's a statistical artifact, not a discovery. High within-group variance from mixing 7 L1 groups reduces statistical power. The finding tells us nothing about NNS writing."

Response: This objection succeeds partially. The authors acknowledge this is part of their point — that treating NNS as a monolith is methodologically problematic precisely because it suppresses real L1-driven variation. But the objection is correct that C-001 should not be read as "NNS writers are syntactically similar to NS writers." It means "heterogeneous pooling hides real differences." The distinction matters. C-001 confidence remains at 55 but the interpretation is constrained: this is a finding about research methodology, not about NNS-NS similarity.

Objection 2: "With 7 groups x 14 measures = 98 pairwise comparisons, finding significance for 'one or more groups' on all 14 measures is near-certain without strict correction. C-002 is inflated."

Response: This objection has force. The abstract does not specify the multiple-comparison correction used for the L1-specific analysis. The discussion snippet mentions results differing from Ai & Lu (2013) but the statistical methodology is paywalled. If the authors used per-measure Bonferroni across the 7 L1 groups (7 comparisons per measure), the per-comparison alpha would be \~.007, which is manageable. If they used no correction, C-002 is inflated. This cannot be resolved without the full text. C-002 confidence stays at 55 but the failure mode is explicitly flagged.

Objection 3: "The paper is 10 years old, uses ICLE 2.0 (data collected in the 1990s-2000s), and the EFL populations are not representative of current L2 writers who have far more exposure to English through digital media. The findings may not generalize to current cohorts."

Response: This objection succeeds. The ICLE 2.0 data predates widespread internet use, social media, and LLM-assisted writing. Current L2 writers at the college level likely have different exposure profiles. The paper's findings describe a specific historical cohort. Generalization to current L2 writers is \[speculative\]. This does not invalidate the methodological insight (aggregation masks L1 differences) but limits direct application of specific syntactic profiles.

## 14\. Steelmanned Synthesis

What survives adversarial review:

1. Syntactic complexity in college-level English argumentative writing differs by L1 background. Pooling NNS writers from different L1s into a single group suppresses detectable differences to 3 of 14 measures; separating by L1 reveals significance across all 14 measures for at least one L1 group. This is a methodological finding about research design (how you group participants determines what you find), not a finding about NNS-NS equivalence.

2. The 14 L2SCA measures span 5 dimensions. The measure-to-dimension mapping has shifted between Lu (2010) and Lu & Ai (2015). The Lu (2010) classification (Types 1-5) is verified from full text. The Lu & Ai (2015) classification (5 renamed dimensions) is stated in the introduction but the specific assignments require Table 1, which is paywalled.

3. The paper's NLI implication establishes that syntactic complexity features can discriminate L1 backgrounds. This finding is relevant to detection systems only as an analogy: if syntactic features discriminate L1, and detection systems use syntactic features, then L1-influenced writing could be differentially flagged. This chain is plausible but untested.

4. The paper frames its investigation as descriptive, not normative. The introduction is explicit about this (SQ-07). Whether the discussion or conclusions introduce normative recommendations is unknown.

What does not survive:

* Any claim that this paper provides evidence about AI-generated text detection.
* The assertion that homogeneous-L1 studies "overstate" NS-NNS gaps.
* Specific claims about which direction Chinese L1 writers differ from NS on any particular measure.
* Confidence scores above 60 for any claim derived from a single cross-sectional study.
* The prior metric glossary dimension assignments (T/S and VP/T were misclassified).
