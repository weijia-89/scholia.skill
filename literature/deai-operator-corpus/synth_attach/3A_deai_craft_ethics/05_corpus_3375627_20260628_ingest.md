## Key Findings / Claims Register

### C-001 — Information transfer model critique

The Shannon-Weaver / information transfer model underlies many AI writing systems and is "badly flawed" because it assumes meaning is fully contained in the data corpus and can be delivered one-way to a passive audience. \[verified — see Q-001, Q-002\]

### C-002 — Social context model as alternative

The authors propose a "social context model" that foregrounds co-creation of meaning by writer and audience, rhetorical exigence, purpose, and the ethical understandings shaping communicative interaction. \[verified — see Q-003\]

### C-003 — Ethic of transparency

Humans must know when they are interacting with an AI agent; this knowledge changes the rhetorical context and micro-ethical understandings for the interaction. \[verified — see Q-004\]

### C-004 — Ethic of critical data awareness

Designers and operators must exercise "methodological reflexivity" about what is and is not in the data — recognizing omissions, biases, malicious agents who skew data, and the limits of data-driven audience analysis. \[verified — see Q-005, Q-006\]

### C-005 — Contextual fluency continuum

AI writing agents exist on a continuum from well-bounded, low-context genres (quarterly earnings reports) to high-context, audience-dependent genres (investigative journalism, nuanced narratives). Performance degrades as contextual complexity increases. \[verified — see Q-007\]

### C-006 — "What is not there" in the data

Rhetorical context — the why, the how, the ethical codes, the prior knowledge, the trust relationships — is typically absent from training corpora but is critical for meaningful and ethical communication. \[verified — see Q-008\]

### C-007 — Delegation vs. collaboration framing

The human-machine relationship may be better described as "delegation" (division of labor with human providing direction, purpose, ethical guidance) than "collaboration" or "symbiosis." The centaur metaphor is proposed. \[verified — see Q-009\]

### C-008 — Reductive audience analysis

Systems like Persado and HireVue perform audience analysis but in reductive ways — collapsing complex human contexts to limited data features (emotional profiles, facial action units). The claim to "objectivity" makes this worse, not better. \[verified — see Q-010\]

### C-009 — Ethical choice in omission (GameChanger)

GameChanger's decision never to say anything negative about a player is framed as a deliberate ethical choice: "being positive vs telling a fuller story." This illustrates how AI writing systems embed normative ethics through what they exclude. \[verified — see Q-011\]

### C-010 — RLHF-adjacent observation (unstated)

The paper does not use the term RLHF or discuss reward-model training. However, the mechanism it describes — where a system learns from "human approval and curation" signals (Reddit karma in GPT-2's training set) and produces text that "feels close to human quality" while missing rhetorical context — structurally parallels the RLHF residue problem. \[inferred: structural parallel, not author-stated. No Gate A quote available for RLHF specifically.\]

## Lane A — deai_removal

Borrowable structural moves for the deai skill:

* The "not there" diagnostic (CM-01): RLHF-trained models generate text without access to rhetorical context (audience identity, exigence, ethical codes, trust relationships). The residue patterns that deai targets — hedging, false warmth, over-qualification, generic coherence — are the model's response to this absence. McKee & Porter provide the theoretical frame: these patterns fill the space where context should be. The deai operator can use this as a principled test: "Is this phrase doing real rhetorical work (addressing a known audience with a specific purpose), or is it filling a context gap the model couldn't access?"
* The transparency audit (CM-04): In mixed human-AI text, the direction of voice normalization matters. The deai lane should always normalize toward operator voice, never toward model voice. This is the practical application of McKee & Porter's transparency ethic.
* Normative vs. empirical separation (CM-02): The deai skill should not treat RLHF residue removal as a moral imperative derived from McKee & Porter's ethics framework. It is a craft operation. The ethics framework provides a lens for understanding why residue exists (models lack context), not a mandate to remove it. The decision to remove is the operator's craft choice.

What this paper does NOT provide to deai:

* No empirical detection methodology. No stylometric features, no classifier results, no corpus analysis.
* No discussion of RLHF, instruction-tuning, or reward models. The structural parallel to RLHF residue (C-010) is inferred, not stated.
* No engagement with the question of whether AI-generated text should be preserved as a legitimate voice. The paper assumes the human must provide ethical guidance; it does not consider cases where the operator deliberately retains AI-contributed patterns.

## Lane B — tic_enrichment

Borrowable structural moves for the tic skill:

* Exigence-naming (CM-03): Before writing, the operator names the rhetorical exigence — the specific imperfection or urgency that calls for this text. This is generative (it produces voice-specific content) and anti-residue (text with genuine exigence reads differently from text that responds to a prompt without one). The tic skill can incorporate this as a pre-writing step.
* Social context model as voice anchor: McKee & Porter's social context model (C-002) positions meaning as co-created between writer and audience within a specific context. For tic enrichment, this means: voice is not a property of the text alone but of the relationship between writer, audience, and situation. The operator's voice emerges from having a specific audience, a specific purpose, and specific ethical commitments — not from stylistic decoration.
* Audience as co-creator: The paper's insistence that meaning is co-created (Q-003) supports the tic principle that voice moves should be calibrated to a real audience, not to an abstract ideal of "good writing." This protects non-native operators from being pushed toward native-speaker prose norms. \[esl_preserve\]

What this paper does NOT provide to tic:

* No specific voice-craft techniques. No sentence-level patterns, no syntactic templates.
* No engagement with L1-influenced voice or multilingual writing contexts.
* The paper's examples (GameChanger, Quakebot, Persado) are all institutional/commercial AI writing, not individual voice-craft scenarios.

## Adversarial Self-Review

Steelman: "AI-assisted voice should be preserved" McKee & Porter's framework actually supports a version of this position they do not articulate. If rhetorical context determines meaning, and if an operator deliberately chooses to retain AI-contributed patterns because those patterns serve the operator's communicative purpose with their specific audience, then the social context model validates that choice. The transparency ethic requires disclosure, not removal. This is a genuine tension the paper does not resolve.

Falsifiers per load-bearing claim:

* C-001 (information transfer model critique): Would be weakened if modern LLMs with massive context windows and instruction-following demonstrate robust rhetorical-context sensitivity without explicit social modeling. Post-2020 models with RLHF and system prompts partially address the "one-way" critique. The critique was stronger against GPT-2 era systems than against current chat models. \[RECENCY RISK — paper is 2020, pre-ChatGPT\]
* C-003 (transparency ethic): Would be falsified if empirical research showed that disclosure of AI involvement degrades communication quality or trust more than non-disclosure. Some recent work on AI disclosure effects suggests complex, context-dependent outcomes. \[RECENCY RISK\]
* C-006 ("not there" in the data): Would be weakened if retrieval-augmented generation or tool-using agents successfully access rhetorical context at inference time. Current RAG systems partially address this. The claim's strength depends on what counts as "rhetorical context" — if narrowly defined (social relationships, trust, ethical codes), it remains strong; if broadly defined (any external information), it is already partially addressed.

Confidence calibration for primary claims:

* C-001: \[c1=75 (Tier 1 venue, rhetoric theory grounded), c2=60 (theoretical argument, no empirical test; partially obsoleted by post-2020 models), c3=55 (strong counter from modern LLM context capabilities)\] FINAL = 60. \[RECENCY RISK\]
* C-003: \[c1=80 (well-established in communication ethics), c2=70 (broadly accepted but empirical evidence on disclosure effects is mixed), c3=65 (counter: some contexts benefit from non-disclosure)\] FINAL = 70.

## Gaps and Unknowns

* Paper has no empirical component. All claims are normative or illustrative.
* No engagement with: RLHF, instruction-tuning, fine-tuning, reward modeling, language model alignment, AI detection, stylometry, or any post-2019 NLP development.
* No engagement with: non-English writing contexts, L1-influenced voice, multilingual AI writing.
* The "rhetorical context" concept is powerful but operationally vague — the paper does not specify how to measure or implement contextual awareness in practice.
* The two ethical principles (transparency, critical data awareness) are stated at a high level without implementation guidance for individual writers (vs. system designers).
