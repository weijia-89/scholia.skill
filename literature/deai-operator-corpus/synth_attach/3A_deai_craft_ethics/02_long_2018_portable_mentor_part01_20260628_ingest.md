## 1) TL;DR

* Concrete diction (sensory nouns, proper names, specific verbs) correlates with higher narrative quality in fiction, but the relationship is genre- and audience-dependent — not a universal lever. The Lexicon Practice and Word Trap are systematic gathering methods Long claims separate "competent" from first-rate writers; the mechanism is practitioner testimony, not controlled evidence.
* Daily timed writing (minimum 15 min, handwritten notebook, no stopping) is a generative practice distinct from revision. Boice (1983) provides experimental evidence that scheduled daily writing triples output vs. spontaneous writing. Long's stronger claim that it "moots writer's block" is unfalsifiable as stated.
* Repetition is a deliberate craft move, not an error. Menninghaus et al. (2017) provide empirical evidence that parallelistic diction (multi-feature recurrence including lexical repetition) intensifies both emotional response and aesthetic judgment across 40 poems.
* Sound work (alliteration, assonance, slant rhyme) operates as subtext. The vowel-scale emotional-register theory (Nims) has partial empirical support (Auracher et al. 2020: formant dispersion predicts Potency and Evaluation, R-squared 4–5%) but was disconfirmed for vowel-frequency-based iconicity by Kraxenberger and Menninghaus (2016). The effect, where it exists, is small.
* Verb strength absorbs adverbs; observation exercises (Here and Now) are concrete-word farms that train sensory perception and feed the lexicon pipeline.

## 7) Unverified claims and evidence gaps

Claims not testable with available tools:

* Whether the Lexicon Practice specifically (vs. voracious reading, vs. general writing instruction) causes diction improvement. Would require controlled longitudinal study with blinding.
* Whether the "rule of three" for repetition (once = accident, twice = confidence, three+ = intention) operates as described in prose readers' perception. Would require reader-response experiment isolating repetition count.
* Whether the Here and Now exercise transfers to improved observational specificity in finished prose. Would require pre/post comparison of writers' published work.
* Whether handwriting advantage extends from learning/encoding tasks to sustained creative composition. All existing studies use short tasks (letter formation, word learning, note-taking).

Evidence that would change the assessment:

* A controlled study comparing Lexicon Practice writers vs. voracious-reader-only writers on diction range measures would either promote C-002 to \[verified\] or kill it.
* A replication of Auracher et al. (2020) in English (not German) poetry would clarify whether the vowel-scale effect crosses languages, which matters for an ESL operator working in English.
* A reader-response study testing isolated lexical repetition (not multi-feature parallelism) in prose would clarify whether CM-03 transfers from the Menninghaus poetry finding.
* Maslej et al. (2019) used online fiction ratings from a writing website (not literary critics). A study using expert literary critics' ratings could resolve whether the abstract-language advantage is audience-specific.
