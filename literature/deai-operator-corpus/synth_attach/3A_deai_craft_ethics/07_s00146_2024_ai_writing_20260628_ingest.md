## Scope and Coverage Attestation

This paper is NOT a detection paper. It is a prompt-engineering framework paper grounded in rhetorical theory (Bitzer 1968). It proposes a formula — Prompt = audience + genre + purpose + subject + context + exigence + writer — tested on GPT-3 (text-davinci-003) across three TPC genres (user guide, white paper, memo).

Detection relevance: indirect. The paper defines what Ranade et al. consider the structural components of "effective" AI-generated text. These components map to detectable signals when absent. The paper does not test any detector, report any detection accuracy, or address ESL bias in detection.

Coverage attestation:

* Attached primary truncated at 80,000 of 125,911 chars. Sections 1-12 are present. Conclusion, full references, and appendix with prompt variations are in the truncated tail.

### Claim-by-Claim Verification

C-001: Rhetorical formula produces linearly better output

Claim (Ranade et al.): Across all genres, total rubric grades increased linearly as prompts included more rhetorical components, with one exception.

Source anchor: Q-009 "Across all outputs, we noticed differences in the structure of the text, degree of context, attention to audience, and style and voice." §8.3. Q-007 "we found the relationship between the intended writer and reader was not clearly defined, leading to a limited output" §8.3.

Gate A: Springer URL 200 OK. Gate B: Claim appears in §8.3.

Tag: \[verified-in-source\] — the authors make this claim. However:

Methodological problems:

* Two raters, \~18 outputs, no inter-rater reliability reported
* No statistical test (no regression, no correlation coefficient)
* "Linear increase" is eyeballed from letter grades (A-D), an ordinal scale treated as interval
* Custom rubric, unvalidated against established writing assessment instruments
* One exception noted but not analyzed — if one of three genres breaks the pattern, that is a 33% failure rate

Confidence: \[c1=35, c2=25, c3=35\] FINAL=30 \[Weak\]

Ceiling applied: Single study, unreplicated, quality issues (underpowered, no pre-registration) = max 70, then reduced for quality to max 40. Eyeballed ordinal data with no test further reduces to 30.

Failure mode: The "linear" pattern is an artifact of grading ordinal letter grades. A→D = 4→1 is arbitrary. Different rubric weighting could produce any pattern from the same outputs.

C-002: All seven rhetorical elements should be present in prompt

Claim: The formula demands all components for optimal output.

Gate A: 200 OK. Gate B: Formula stated §8.1, but "demands all components" is aspirational, not empirically isolated.

Tag: \[verified-in-source\] — formula is stated. The "all seven necessary" claim is \[speculative\] — no ablation study, no element-by-element contribution analysis.

Confidence: \[c1=35, c2=25, c3=40\] FINAL=35 \[Weak\]

Failure mode: Any single element could be doing most of the work (e.g., audience alone could account for the improvement). Without ablation, the "all seven" claim is unfalsifiable from presented data.

Confidence: \[c1=20, c2=15, c3=25\] FINAL=20 \[Speculative\]

Failure mode: Specifying audience could narrow bias (toward that audience's norms) rather than reduce it. The claim assumes bias reduction = good, but audience-specific framing can introduce new biases (stereotyping the specified audience).

### Craft Move Verification Against External Detection Literature

move-001 (absence_of_audience_orientation)

External corroboration sought: Do actual AI detectors use "audience orientation" as a feature?

Result: No mainstream detector (GPTZero, ZeroGPT, Turnitin, DetectGPT) uses audience orientation as an explicit feature. Detectors rely on perplexity, burstiness, token probability, and trained classifiers. \[EQ-12, EQ-13\]

However: The concept of "absence of audience" as a structural property of AI text does appear in practitioner/theoretical literature — VERMILLION framework lists "Lack of personal voice/authorial presence" \[EQ-9\], and discourse/pragmatic cues (stance, engagement) are identified as a detection cue family in the rapid review at doi.org/10.3390/bdcc10020055 \[EQ-11\].

Tag: \[inferred:detection-literature\] — the inference chain is: (1) Ranade et al. identify audience as a prompt element \[verified-in-source\]; (2) external detection literature identifies stance/engagement as detection-relevant cue family \[verified — EQ-11\]; (3) therefore absence of audience orientation is a plausible deai_signal. But no detector has been shown to operationalize this specific feature.

Confidence: FINAL=35 \[Weak\]

move-002 (context_vacuum)

External corroboration: Context specificity appears in the rapid review's "Epistemic/Content" cue family \[EQ-11\] as "grounding, truthfulness, and experience." TextSight notes detectors can "misflag polished human writing" when context is formal/constrained, suggesting context-level features are indirectly captured by perplexity.

Tag: \[inferred:detection-literature\] — plausible but operationally vague.

Confidence: FINAL=35 \[Weak\]

move-003 (missing_exigence)

External corroboration sought: No detection paper, framework, or detector uses "exigence" or "reason to write" as a feature. The VERMILLION framework's closest analog is "absence of lived experience," which is related but distinct.

Tag: \[speculative\] — upgraded from \[inferred:detection-literature\]. No external support found. This is the most novel and least grounded of the four craft moves.

Confidence: FINAL=20 \[Speculative\]

Failure mode: "Exigence" operates at the document or rhetorical-situation level — far above the sentence/token level where detectors operate. A text can have clear exigence and still be detected as AI-generated, or lack exigence and read as human.

move-004 (omniscient_no_author)

External corroboration: This is the strongest of the four. VERMILLION explicitly lists "Lack of personal voice/authorial presence (minimal first-person usage; impersonal tone)" as an AI marker \[EQ-9\]. The ERM framework finds "Performed hesitancy markers appear at about twice the density in LLM outputs compared to humans" — meaning AI text performs epistemic markers (hedges) but in a calibration-mismatched way \[EQ-10\]. The rapid review identifies "Discourse/Pragmatic: stance, engagement, metadiscourse" as a detection cue family \[EQ-11\].

Tag: \[inferred\] — chain: (1) Ranade et al. include "writer" as formula element \[verified-in-source\]; (2) VERMILLION identifies absence of authorial presence as AI marker \[verified — EQ-9\]; (3) ERM framework quantifies epistemic marker miscalibration in LLM text \[verified — EQ-10\]; (4) therefore writer positionality absence is a plausible and externally corroborated deai_signal.

Confidence: FINAL=45 \[Weak-to-Moderate\]

Ceiling: VERMILLION is a single heuristic framework (not peer-reviewed in the traditional sense). ERM is a preprint (arxiv 2604.19768). Neither provides detector accuracy metrics for this specific feature. Cap at 45.

## Craft Moves (Post-Review)

### move-001

* lane: deai_removal
* structural_borrow: When removing AI residue, check whether the text lacks audience specification — generic/unaddressed audience is a plausible AI signal. Human writers inherently write for someone; AI text defaults to universal address.
* anti_pastiche: Do not mechanically insert audience markers. The borrow is structural: ensure the text has an implicit or explicit audience orientation, not formulaic address.
* \[esl_preserve\]: Audience orientation can manifest through L1-influenced syntax (e.g., topic-prominent structures common in Chinese L1 writers). This is legitimate voice. Do not "fix" it. WARNING: Liang et al. 2023 show detectors disproportionately flag ESL writing (61% false-positive rate). Adding formulaic audience markers could worsen this. Implement structurally, not formulaically.
* deai_signal: absence_of_audience_orientation
* source_anchor: Q-001, Q-004
* confidence: \[inferred:detection-literature\] FINAL=35. External corroboration: discourse/pragmatic cues identified as detection-relevant cue family (doi.org/10.3390/bdcc10020055). No detector operationalizes this specific feature.

### move-002

* lane: deai_removal
* structural_borrow: Context specificity as de-AI move. When editing AI-generated text, add context-specific details (role, situation, constraints) that a human writer would naturally include.
* anti_pastiche: Do not over-specify. Human writing often leaves context implicit. Match human-natural levels of specificity.
* \[esl_preserve\]: L1 Chinese writers may use high-context communication patterns (less explicit context, more reliance on shared understanding). This is a valid rhetorical choice, not under-specification.
* deai_signal: context_vacuum
* source_anchor: Q-005, C-004
* confidence: \[inferred:detection-literature\] FINAL=35. Operationally vague — "context specificity" is hard to measure.

### move-003

* lane: deai_removal
* structural_borrow: Exigence as authenticity marker. Human writing responds to a reason to write. AI text often lacks exigence. When revising, surface genuine exigence already present in the writing situation.
* anti_pastiche: Do not fabricate urgency.
* \[esl_preserve\]: N/A — discourse-level feature.
* deai_signal: missing_exigence
* source_anchor: Q-003, Q-010
* confidence: \[speculative\] FINAL=20. No external detection literature uses "exigence" or "reason to write" as a feature. Most novel and least grounded move. Operates at document level, far above detector resolution. May not appear as load-bearing in synthesis.

### move-004

* lane: deai_removal
* structural_borrow: Writer positionality. AI text speaks from an omniscient, institutional nowhere. Injecting writer positionality (first person, role acknowledgment, epistemic stance) is a de-AI move.
* anti_pastiche: Do not force first-person if genre doesn't warrant it. Ensure discernible author position, even if implicit.
* \[esl_preserve\]: L1-influenced epistemic hedging (e.g., "I think perhaps..." patterns common in Chinese academic English) is legitimate writer positionality. Do not flatten to Western-assertive style. WARNING per Liang et al. 2023: detectors may flag ESL epistemic hedging patterns. Do not add performed hesitancy markers — AI text already overuses them (ERM framework: performed hesitancy at 2x human density, arxiv 2604.19768).
* deai_signal: omniscient_no_author
* source_anchor: Q-001, Q-006
* confidence: \[inferred\] FINAL=45. Strongest move. External corroboration: VERMILLION framework identifies "Lack of personal voice/authorial presence" as AI marker. ERM framework quantifies epistemic marker miscalibration. Rapid review identifies discourse/pragmatic stance as detection cue family.

## Steelman Synthesis

The strongest version of findings that survives adversarial review:

Ranade et al. 2025 provide a rhetorical vocabulary — not empirical evidence — for describing what AI-generated text lacks. The formula (audience + genre + purpose + subject + context + exigence + writer) maps to four proposed deai_signals, of which three have partial external corroboration and one (exigence) does not. The strongest signal is omniscient_no_author (FINAL=45), corroborated by the VERMILLION framework and ERM preprint. The weakest is missing_exigence (FINAL=20), with no external support.

These signals are useful as human-reviser heuristics during deai_removal passes. They are not detector features and should not be treated as empirically validated detection signals. They complement existing RLHF-residue signals (which describe what AI text contains) by describing what AI text omits.

The paper's empirical claims are weak (FINAL 20-35 across all tested claims). Its theoretical contribution — applying Bitzer's rhetorical situation to the question "what makes text read as human-authored?" — is the actual borrowable, and that contribution comes from Bitzer 1968, not from Ranade et al.'s experiments.

ESL constraint: Any implementation must account for Liang et al. 2023's finding that detectors disproportionately flag non-native writing. Craft moves that increase textual polish or add formulaic markers risk worsening this bias.
