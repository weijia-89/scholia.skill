## 2\. Chapter Summary

Ch12 treats the résumé as a persuasive genre — not a record but a document designed to survive two gates: machine scan and human skim (10–30 seconds). The chapter covers:

* Job-hunt timeline (informal campus prep → formal year-before-graduation cycle)
* Self-assessment as prerequisite to résumé content
* Internet job search mechanics and risks (phishing, cached data)
* Personal branding across LinkedIn, blogs, Twitter, Facebook, professional forums
* Networking as reciprocal relationship, not extraction
* Social media hygiene (removing unprofessional content before job market)
* Internships as hiring pipeline (\~58% conversion rate per NACE 2010; textbook rounds to 57%)
* How employers use résumés (scan test → skim test → approval chain)
* Guidelines: length, emphasis, details, writing style, key words, layout/design
* Résumé types: chronological vs. skills/functional
* Content sections: name/contact, career objective, summary of qualifications (truncation begins mid-section)

Core thesis: The résumé is not autobiography — it is targeted persuasion under extreme time and space constraints. Every element must earn its space by advancing the candidate's fit for a specific position.

## 4\. Key Concepts Extracted

### 4a. Résumé as persuasion, not record \[lane: both\]

The chapter's foundational move: reframing the résumé from informational document to persuasive one. "You cannot lie, but you can omit some information that does not work in your favor." This is genre awareness — understanding that résumé conventions permit strategic omission in ways that other professional documents (application forms) do not.

Craft relevance: This persuasion-within-constraint frame maps onto de-AI work. RLHF-trained models produce text that hedges everything and omits nothing — the opposite of what this chapter teaches. The résumé genre demands ruthless selection. \[deai_removal\]

### 4b. Two-gate filtering: scan test + skim test \[lane: both\]

Résumés must pass machine keyword scanning AND human 10–30 second skim. These impose contradictory demands:

* Scan test rewards noun density, keyword repetition, synonyms
* Skim test rewards visual hierarchy, white space, emphasis positioning

Craft relevance: Analogous to writing that must satisfy both algorithmic detection avoidance and human reader engagement. The dual-audience problem is structural, not cosmetic. \[deai_removal\]

Note: The scan-test mechanics described in the textbook (2012-era keyword matching) are outdated. Modern ATS systems use NLP-based parsing that reduces the keyword-stuffing imperative. The structural insight (dual-audience constraint) remains transferable; the tactical advice does not. \[RECENCY RISK\]

### 4c. Concision mechanics \[lane: tic_enrichment\]

Three specific concision techniques taught:

1. Consolidation: Collapse repeated entries into single lines with date ranges
  * Wordy: four separate "Member, Meat Judging Team" lines → Tight: one line with range + Captain notation
2. Verb form selection: The textbook claims active verbs > gerunds > nouns "for dynamism"
  * "Calculated foundation loads" beats "Performed foundation load calculations"
3. Pronoun elimination: No "I" — forces sentence-fragment construction that is inherently tighter

Moves 1 and 3 are concrete, teachable compression techniques. Move 2 (verb hierarchy) requires scope limitation — see adversarial findings in Section 7.

### 4d. Detail calibration \[lane: tic_enrichment\]

The chapter provides a clear decision rule for detail inclusion: details must either (a) add evidence beyond what the title alone conveys, or (b) include quantified specifics (numbers of people, dollar amounts, percentage improvements). If details are less impressive than the title alone, omit them.

Example pair from text:

* Too vague: "Supervised staff; promoted ad sales."
* Good details: "Supervised 22-member sales staff; helped recruit, interview, and select staff; assigned duties and scheduled work; recommended best performers for promotion. Motivated staff to increase paid ad inches 10% over previous year's sales."

Craft relevance: This is the "earn your space" principle applied at the detail level. Directly useful for tic_enrichment — the question "does this detail strengthen or dilute?" is a universal editing heuristic.

### 4e. Emphasis positioning \[lane: tic_enrichment\]

Explicit instruction on document-level emphasis:

* Top and bottom of page are positions of emphasis
* Put less important material in the middle of lists, not at the end
* Avoid "fading out" — ending on weak items
* Use informative headings that name skills from job ad
* Vertical lists emphasize; buried prose de-emphasizes

This is visual rhetoric applied to a one-page document. The "fading out" warning is a transferable principle for any sequential writing.

### 4f. Keyword strategy \[lane: both\]

Key words are "frequently nouns or noun phrases" — which creates tension with the writing style advice to prefer verbs and gerunds. The chapter resolves this by recommending keyword density in the Summary of Qualifications section while maintaining verb-driven prose in Experience sections.

Additional keyword tactics:

* Mirror the exact language of the specific job ad
* Spell out professional organizations AND include abbreviations
* Use synonyms: "manager and management, Excel and spreadsheets"
* Include area codes for geographic filtering

Note: The noun-phrase keyword emphasis has been partially validated by the Ajusta ATS study (2024), which found "nouns, tools, technologies, and domain terms following the verb drive keyword and skills scoring." The tactical advice to mirror job-ad language remains sound across ATS generations. \[RECENCY RISK on ATS mechanics; keyword-mirroring principle survives\]

### 4g. Layout rhetoric \[lane: tic_enrichment\]

* Headings placement: left-margin with text below (for dense content) vs. left-column headings with right-column text (spreads thin content)
* Three-font maximum
* Minimum 10pt for large fonts (Arial), 11pt for smaller fonts (Times New Roman)
* Color sparingly or not at all — interferes with scanning
* White 8.5x11 paper remains standard
* Template avoidance: employers recognize and discount Word templates

### 4h. Résumé types: chronological vs. skills/functional \[lane: both\]

| Type | Best when | Emphasizes |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Chronological | Education/experience align with target job; impressive titles/dates | Timeline, titles, progression |
| Skills/functional | Changing fields; non-traditional path; combining diverse experience sources | Transferable capabilities regardless of source |

The skills résumé is explicitly recommended for career changers, gap returners, and those combining paid/volunteer/academic experience. \[esl_preserve — particularly relevant for international candidates whose career trajectories don't follow US chronological norms\]

Note: An Accountemps survey of Canadian executives found 61% prefer chronological format. This creates tension with the textbook's functional-résumé recommendation for non-traditional candidates. The preference data suggests functional résumés carry a format-penalty risk even when content warrants them. \[speculative — single survey, limited geography\]
