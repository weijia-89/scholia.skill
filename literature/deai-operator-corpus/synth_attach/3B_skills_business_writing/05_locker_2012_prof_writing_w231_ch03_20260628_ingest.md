## 2\. Core Constructs and Definitions

### You-Attitude

A style of communication that looks at things from the audience's point of view, emphasizing what the audience wants to know, respecting the audience's intelligence, and protecting the audience's ego.

Five sentence-level techniques:

1. Talk about the audience, not about yourself
2. Refer to the customer's request or order specifically
3. Don't talk about feelings, except to congratulate or offer sympathy
4. In positive situations, use "you" more often than "I"; use "we" when it includes the audience
5. In negative situations, avoid "you"; use passive verbs and impersonal expressions to avoid assigning blame

Beyond sentence level — content strategies:

* Be complete (appendix for dense detail)
* Anticipate and answer likely audience questions
* Show how the subject affects the audience

Beyond sentence level — organization strategies:

* Put audience-priority information first
* Arrange for audience needs, not writer needs
* Use headings and lists for scannability

### Positive Emphasis

Five techniques:

1. Avoid negative words and words with negative connotations (see Figure 3.3 negative-word list)
2. Beware of hidden negatives ("but," "however," "I hope," "I trust," "patience")
3. Focus on what the audience can do rather than on limitations
4. Justify negative information by giving a reason or linking to an audience benefit
5. Put negative information in the middle and present it compactly

Three checks on positive emphasis:

* Appropriateness: some bad news (layoffs, defects, salary cuts) requires straightforward negatives
* Sincerity: overdone positivity reads as insincerity
* Realism: excessive praise clouds reality and demotivates top performers

### Tone, Power, and Politeness

Tone = implied attitude of communicator toward audience. Key insight: tone interacts with context and power. Language acceptable within one group may be unacceptable from outside it. Words friendly from superior to subordinate may seem "uppity" from subordinate to superior.

Politeness gradient (low to high):

1. Order: "Turn in your time card by Monday."
2. Polite order: "Please turn in your time card by Monday."
3. Indirect request: "Time cards should be turned in by Monday."
4. Question: "Would you be able to turn in your time card by Monday?"

Calibration rule: more politeness needed when request inconveniences audience and benefits requester. Less politeness when request is small, routine, or benefits audience.

### Reducing Bias (partial attestation)

Bias-free language: language that does not discriminate on the basis of sex, physical condition, race, ethnicity, age, religion, or any other category.

Nonsexist language — four areas checked:

* Job titles (use neutral: firefighter, server, supervisor, mail carrier)
* Courtesy titles and names (Mr./Ms. default; professional title when applicable; respect stated preference per you-attitude)
* Pronouns (truncated — technique details not fully attested)
* Other words and phrases (truncated)

## 7\. Feeling-Attribution Contrast (post-review)

This chapter's core deai relevance: the distinction between feeling-talk and fact-talk.

Locker's rule: don't tell the audience how they feel ("You'll be happy to hear..." — Q-004), and don't tell the audience how you feel ("We are happy to..." — Q-003). Both are you-attitude failures that center the wrong party.

RLHF-trained models produce structurally identical patterns:

* Model-feeling attribution: "I'm happy to help!" / "Great question!" / "I'm glad you asked."
* Audience-feeling attribution: "You'll be pleased to know..." / "You might find it interesting that..."

Locker's alternative: state the fact. Let the audience determine their own emotional response. "Your scholarship has been renewed" over "You'll be happy to hear that your scholarship has been renewed."

Post-review correction: The initial ingest called this an "independent confirmation" of the deai canon. That overclaims. Locker's framework addresses a different problem (writer-centrism in persuasive business communication) than the deai canon addresses (reward-model sycophancy in LLM output). The surface patterns are identical. The recommended fix transfers. But the frameworks are independent only in the trivial sense that Locker predates RLHF. They do not confirm each other because they operate on different causal models. Accurate framing: Locker provides a pedagogical warrant from business-communication tradition for a fix the deai canon already recommends. The warrant is useful (it answers "why does stripping these phrases improve communication?" from a human-communication-theory perspective) but it is not a second data point on RLHF behavior.

\[esl_preserve\] note: L1 Chinese speakers may use feeling-attribution constructions that mirror polite Chinese discourse patterns (e.g., formal written Chinese 很高兴通知您 "very glad to inform you"). When this appears in operator text, do not flag it as RLHF residue. Diagnostic: did the operator write it, or did the model generate it? If the operator wrote it, preserve it. If the model generated it, remove it. Note: this diagnostic is operator-derived, not Locker-warranted. Locker does not discuss L1 transfer.
