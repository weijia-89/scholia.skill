## Craft-Move Extraction

### CM-01: Information control via naming (give-and-take)

* Lane: tic_enrichment
* Source: Olmstead LESSON on Melville
* Core: "He tells you what to call him, not what his name is." (Q-002) The opening controls what the reader receives. The writer decides what to withhold and what to offer. "He gives and he takes, all for the purpose of spinning his yarn." (Q-004)
* Mechanism: Selective disclosure — assert one thing while explicitly denying another. Creates authority and curiosity simultaneously.
* deai application: \[inferred from canon\] RLHF-trained prose defaults to full disclosure. This move teaches strategic omission. Connection depends on C-002.
* \[esl_preserve\]: L1 Chinese rhetorical tradition includes delayed-thesis patterns (qi-cheng-zhuan-he). This move is compatible with indirection as craft. \[inferred from typology, not from Olmstead\]
* Implementation gate: Test in target domain (expository/technical) before codifying.

### CM-02: Intimacy calibration (reader-narrator distance)

* Lane: tic_enrichment
* Source: Olmstead WRITING POSSIBILITIES §3 on Melville
* Core: "think about how intimate you want that voice to be to the listener, about the relationship you want between your character and your reader."
* Mechanism: Physical metaphor for rhetorical distance — across the table, whispering, arm around shoulder. Conscious proximity selection.
* deai application: \[inferred from canon\] RLHF outputs default to a single register (helpful assistant at medium distance). Conscious distance-setting lets the writer own a specific stance. Dependency: C-002.

### CM-03: Declarative anchor + flight pattern

* Lane: tic_enrichment
* Source: Olmstead LESSON on Brodkey
* Core: Brodkey opens with "It starts in my sleep" — a simple declaration — then moves through speculation, memory, metaphor. Olmstead: "In these few lines he remembers, he speculates, he declares, he recounts, he reveals, and he makes wisdom." (Q-008)
* Mechanism: Plant a factual anchor sentence. Use it as launchpad for associative movement. The return-to-ground is visible in Brodkey's text (sentence 4 references sentence 1's dream) but is not explicitly named by Olmstead as a pattern. \[inferred — C-011\]
* deai application: \[inferred from canon\] RLHF prose enumerates or explains rather than taking associative flight. This move licenses digression-from-anchor. Dependency: C-002.
* Implementation gate: Anchor sentence must be concrete and verifiable. Test in target domain before codifying. Risk of incoherence if anchor is weak.

### CM-04: Two-word sentence as condition-setter

* Lane: both (deai_removal + tic_enrichment)
* Source: Olmstead LESSON on Simpson
* Core: "This novel begins with a two-word sentence—a subject and a verb." (Q-010) "We fought." establishes condition, not event. Names an ongoing state.
* Mechanism: Minimal syntax creates maximum interpretive space. Subject + verb alone forces the reader to fill context.
* deai application (deai_removal): \[inferred from canon, scope-qualified\] RLHF prose in default/unprompted output avoids ultra-short declaratives. Two-word openings are structurally oppositional. Dependency: C-005.
* deai application (tic_enrichment): Short declaratives signal compression and confidence. \[inferred\]
* Implementation gate: Situational, not default. Olmstead presents as one possibility, not best practice. Test in target domain.

### CM-05: Information burial via adjective placement

* Lane: both (deai_removal + tic_enrichment)
* Source: Olmstead LESSON on Simpson
* Core: "this information is revealed in an adjective, not in a statement of fact" (Q-012). Simpson buries the stolen-car information inside a prepositional phrase inside a clause. "That the car is stolen is made to be much less important than that they are fighting." (Q-011)
* Mechanism: Hierarchize information syntactically. Grammar decides importance, not explicit announcement.
* deai application (deai_removal): \[inferred from canon\] RLHF prose foregrounds everything equally — important information gets standalone sentences. Burying info in adjectives inverts this pattern. Dependency: C-002.
* deai application (tic_enrichment): Trust move — assumes the reader is paying attention. \[inferred\]
* \[esl_preserve\]: Chinese topic-comment structure is compatible with modifier embedding, but the mechanisms differ. Parataxis (Chinese) ≠ hypotactic subordination (Simpson's English). The \[esl_preserve\] flag protects against treating modifier placement as ESL error; it does not claim syntactic equivalence. \[inferred — C-003, weakened from original\]
* Implementation gate: Test in target domain. Expository prose may require explicit foregrounding for comprehension — burial works in narrative but may fail in technical writing.

### CM-06: Fact-then-detail rhythm

* Lane: tic_enrichment
* Source: Olmstead LESSON on Simpson
* Core: "each time the narrator makes a statement of fact, she follows it with an example, reason or detail." (Q-013) "I was mad. I was mad about a lot of things."
* Mechanism: Assertion → support as rhythmic sequence. Whether this is rhythm or logic is interpretive — Olmstead describes the sequence without naming it as either. \[inferred — C-013\]
* deai application: \[inferred from canon\] RLHF prose uses claim-then-support mechanically (claim → explanation → caveat). Simpson's version is childlike, emotional, recursive. The tic_enrichment move: choose details that reveal stance rather than provide comprehensive coverage. Dependency: C-002.

### CM-07: "Would" as habitual past (relentlessness marker)

* Lane: tic_enrichment
* Source: Olmstead LESSON on Simpson
* Core: "'Would' is the past tense of will and indicates habitual action, in this case, the relentlessness of the fighting." (Q-014)
* Mechanism: Modal verb choice encodes temporal texture. "Would" compresses repeated events into a single grammatical gesture. \[verified — C-012\]
* deai application: \[inferred from canon\] RLHF prose prefers explicit temporal markers ("repeatedly," "often"). Using "would" for habituality is a compression move. Dependency: C-002.
* \[esl_preserve\]: Mandarin lacks morphological tense; habitual aspect is marked lexically (常常, 总是). \[inferred from typology — C-006\] "Would" for habitual past is a documented L2 acquisition challenge. Flag as craft aspiration, not correction. An ESL writer choosing explicit temporal markers is not making an error — they are using a legitimate strategy.

## Steelmanned Synthesis

Seven craft moves extracted from craft01, each grounded in verbatim quotes from the source text. The three moves with the sharpest mechanisms — selective disclosure (CM-01), information burial (CM-05), and two-word anchoring (CM-04) — share a common principle: the writer controls what the reader receives by controlling syntax, not by announcing importance. This principle is the durable extraction from this chapter.

All three are structurally oppositional to patterns observed in RLHF-trained output \[inferred from canon, not verified experimentally\]. All three operate at the sentence level, making them concrete and immediately testable in draft prose. None has been validated outside literary domains.

The ingest adds four moves to the skill canon (gated on domain-transfer testing), changes one (CM-06 reclassification), and keeps two as reference/monitor items. The \[esl_preserve\] tags protect against three specific overcorrection risks: treating delayed thesis as deficiency, treating modifier placement as error, and pushing "would" for habitual past on writers who use explicit temporal markers. These protections are derived from linguistic typology and operator protocol, not from Olmstead.

The chapter's limitation is its own: it teaches by successful example only, provides no failure analysis, and offers imitation exercises with no transfer mechanism. The craft moves are extractable; their generalizability is not demonstrated.
