2. Chapter Summary
Ch08 covers the full lifecycle of job application letters: employer research, the hidden job market (information and referral interviews), the two canonical letter types (solicited vs. prospecting), paragraph-level organization patterns, writing style conventions (positive emphasis, you-attitude, paragraph unity), e-mail adaptations, and professional image standards. The chapter is prescriptive and example-heavy, built around annotated sample letters (Figures 13.4, 13.6, 13.7, 13.8).
Core argument: a job application letter is a persuasive claim — you are arguing that your specific qualifications match a specific job at a specific company. Generic letters fail. The chapter enforces this through its two-type taxonomy and its insistence on company-specific evidence in every letter body.

3b. Organization Patterns
Solicited (Figure 13.3):
	•	Apply for position; state source; summarize qualifications in ad's order
	•	Develop major qualifications with specifics
	•	Develop secondary qualifications; demonstrate company knowledge
	•	Ask for interview; availability; positive close
Prospecting (Figure 13.5):
	•	Catch reader's interest (not a job ask)
	•	Bridge attention-getter → qualifications summary; specify desired niche
	•	Develop strengths with specifics; relate past work to company needs; show company knowledge
	•	Ask for interview; availability (don't state start date); positive close
3c. Hidden Job Market
	•	Chapter claims "as high as 80%" of jobs are unadvertised, attributed to "some authorities" with no primary citation
	•	This is a zombie statistic. The 80% figure traces to Bernard Haldane (1966), who cited a Ford Foundation-funded Rochester NY pilot study by the National Industrial Conference Board. That study found 25.1% of jobs at 27 companies appeared in newspapers; filtering to "career-type roles" yields ~18.9% advertised, implying ~81% unadvertised. The Rochester companies had an agreement not to advertise in newspapers, invalidating generalization. [verified — Preston 2023, Learning Curves, Gate A: 200 OK, Gate B: CLAIM IN SOURCE — exact quotes from Rochester survey reproduced]
	•	Two methods: information interviews (learn about field) and referral interviews (learn about openings, get face-to-face with hiring authority)
	•	Referral chain: start with known contacts → get referrals → reach decision-makers
	•	Never directly ask for a job in referral interviews; let the resume review create the match
	•	Chapter warns: many businesspeople are cynical about info/referral interviews and resent the time; preparation is essential
	•	Networking as job-finding mechanism has partial empirical support via Granovetter (1973, AJS) on weak ties (N=64, Boston suburb), with large-scale causal replication by Rajkumar et al. (2022, Science) on LinkedIn (20M+ users) showing nonlinear/inverted-U relationship between tie weakness and job mobility. The chapter's qualitative claim that networking produces jobs is directionally supported; the specific 80% magnitude is not. [verified — Rajkumar et al. 2022 via Science DOI, Gate A: 200 OK, Gate B: CLAIM IN SOURCE]

3e. Professional Image Checklist
	•	Word-process; standard font (Times New Roman, Arial, Helvetica), 12pt
	•	Address to specific person; verify courtesy title
	•	No relative name-dropping; OK to use known/respected contacts with permission
	•	Omit irrelevant personal info
	•	Conservative style: no contractions, fragments, cliches, slang (unless creative field)
	•	Proof multiple times; double-check receiver's name spelling
	•	Print on matching resume paper; computer-print envelope
	•	Sign with blue or black ink (for physical letters)
3f. E-mail Application Letters

	•	Prospecting: shorter; hook within first screen

	•	Max 65 chars per line

6. Craft-Move Extraction
Move 1: Specificity-over-claim [lane: both]
The chapter's central pedagogical move: every qualification claim must be grounded in concrete detail (three to five sentences). Unsupported claims "sound overconfident, selfish, or arrogant" (Q-004).
deai relevance: AI-generated cover letters consistently produce the failure mode this chapter warns against — vague, inflated claims. The causal mechanism (RLHF vs. training data vs. prompt design) is unresolved [C-004, confidence 55], but the observable pattern is robust across practitioner reports. The chapter's specificity mandate is a direct deai_removal tool: replace abstraction with evidence.
tic relevance: Voice priming benefits from the same discipline. A tic that says "I built X, which did Y" is structurally stronger than "I have extensive experience in Z." Borrow the pattern, not the textbook's voice.
Confidence in transferability: 60 [C-006].
Move 2: Ad-language mirroring [lane: tic_enrichment]
"Be sure your letter uses the exact language of the job ad" (Q-007). The chapter frames this as both human-reader expectation and computer keyword compliance.
ATS caveat (post-review): The computer-keyword rationale is now platform-dependent. Workday, Greenhouse, and Lever do not score cover letter keywords. Taleo and SAP SuccessFactors do, partially. The human-reader rationale — that hiring managers expect to see their ad's vocabulary reflected — remains untested but plausible as practitioner consensus. [C-005, confidence 45 for universal ATS claim; 65 for qualified claim]
tic application: When priming voice for a specific audience, mirror their published language rather than paraphrasing into your own register. This is structural borrowing — adopt their frame, fill it with your evidence. The move transfers beyond ATS contexts to any audience-aware writing.
Move 3: I-density reduction [lane: both]
The chapter provides a concrete toolkit for reducing first-person pronoun saturation: prepositional phrase openers ("Under my presidency..."), introductory clauses ("While I was in Italy..."), switching I to me/my in object position.
deai relevance: AI-generated models over-produce "I" sentences in cover letter and personal statement generation. This toolkit is directly implementable as a revision pass. [C-012, confidence 55]
tic relevance: [esl_preserve] — L1 Chinese speakers may produce topic-prominent sentences that reduce I-density through different mechanisms (e.g., topic-comment structure). These should be preserved when communicatively clear, not "corrected" to English subject-prominent patterns. However, higher-proficiency L2 users may have already moved past TP features [C-007 — Yang & Li 2012 show proficiency-dependent TP decline]. The chapter's toolkit adds options without mandating a single syntactic path.
Move 4: Positive reframing without dishonesty [lane: deai_removal]
The chapter distinguishes between omitting negatives (legitimate) and fabricating positives (not addressed, but implied as illegitimate by the specificity mandate). The "excessive amount" → concrete coursework revision is the model: don't deny the negative, replace it with a specific positive.
deai relevance: AI-generated models often produce false-positive hedging ("While I may not have direct experience, I am eager to learn..."). The chapter's approach: if you don't have the experience, don't mention it. If you do, name it concretely. No apologetic bridges.
Limitation: The chapter's blanket "omit all negatives" advice conflicts with contexts requiring honest limitation acknowledgment. Disposition: CHANGE — keep "replace with specifics" half; operator judgment on omission.
Move 5: Two-type structural selection [lane: tic_enrichment]
Solicited = direct request; prospecting = problem-solving persuasion. The chapter teaches rhetorical situation analysis before drafting: identify whether your audience expects you (solicited) or you must create the expectation (prospecting). [C-013, confidence 60]
tic application: This maps to any persuasive writing context. A pitch to an editor who solicited your work differs structurally from a cold pitch. The two-type taxonomy is a transferable frame.

9. Hostile Expert Objections
Objection 1: "This ingest treats a 2012 undergraduate textbook as a source of craft theory. The chapter is pedagogical, not empirical. It provides zero outcome data on whether its recommended patterns produce better hiring results."
Response: Sustained. The chapter is a prescriptive source, not an evidence-based one. All craft moves extracted should be tagged as pedagogical convention [C-009]. The ingest explicitly marks this: no sample letter has been validated against hiring outcomes. The specificity-over-claim move (Move 1) has partial external support from Brandt & Herzberg 2020, but that study used LIWC categories, not Locker's specific recommendations. No claim in this ingest should be treated as evidence that Locker's advice produces measurably better outcomes.
Objection 2: "The deai_removal lane claims these moves counter RLHF patterns, but the chapter predates RLHF by a decade. The connection is post-hoc and cherry-picked."
Response: Partially sustained. The chapter cannot counter RLHF because it was written before RLHF existed. The connection is that the failure modes the chapter identifies (vague claims, I-saturation, generic language) happen to overlap with patterns observed in AI-generated cover letters. This overlap is real but coincidental, not causal. The ingest should not claim the chapter "directly counters" RLHF — it should claim the chapter's craft moves address the same surface symptoms through different theoretical reasoning. Craft moves retagged accordingly in Move 1 and Move 3.
Objection 3: "The esl_preserve flags are applied without evidence that the operator's actual writing exhibits L1 topic-prominence. You're making assumptions about a person's syntax based on their L1 background, which is itself a form of linguistic profiling."
Response: Sustained as a caution. The esl_preserve flags should be framed as protective defaults (don't correct TP structures if they appear), not assumptions that TP structures will appear. Yang & Li 2012 show that TP features decline with proficiency. The operator's proficiency level is unknown to this ingest. Corrected: esl_preserve flags now say "preserve if present" rather than "expect."
