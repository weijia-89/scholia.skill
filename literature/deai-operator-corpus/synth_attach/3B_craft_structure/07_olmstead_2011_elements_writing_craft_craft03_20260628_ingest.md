## Craft-Move Extraction (deai + tic relevance tagged)

### Move 1 — Efficient scene-setting via sensory fragment \[Lane C both\]

Olmstead on Slater: "The writing begins by efficiently establishing time and place." Season + temperature + location descriptor in a single sentence fragment. No throat-clearing.

deai relevance: RLHF models over-narrate scene entry ("As the sun cast its golden rays..."). This move provides a pattern for direct entry. \[esl_preserve\]: fragment-style opening is legitimate in L1 Chinese rhetorical structure (topic-prominent); do not flag as "incomplete sentence."

### Move 2 — Aftermath entry (bypass the unnarrable) \[Lane A deai_removal\]

Olmstead on Abbott: "A good place to start might be once the dust has settled." The narrator deflects from the incomprehensible event to a small, parallel loss (dreamlife instead of marriage).

deai relevance: RLHF outputs tend to narrate trauma linearly with emotional escalation ("devastating," "heart-wrenching"). Aftermath entry sidesteps this. The move is: name the large thing in a subordinate clause, give the sentence's main weight to the small parallel thing.

### Move 3 — Short-sentence intensity (gunshots / breathlessness) \[Lane B tic_enrichment\]

Olmstead on Chapple: "The intensity of the moment is revealed in these short sentences, like gunshots or breathlessness." Compress peak-action passages into subject-verb chains. Strip modifiers.

tic relevance: structural rhythm borrow. Sentence-length keyed to dramatic intensity. \[esl_preserve\]: short declarative chains are natural in Chinese-influenced English; do not "elaborate."

### Move 4 — Agentless passive for inevitability \[Lane A deai_removal\]

Olmstead on Prejean: "a metal cap is placed, an electrode is fastened." Actor (guards) established once, then dropped. Actions become impersonal, mechanical, inevitable.

deai relevance: RLHF models avoid passive voice reflexively. This move reclaims passive construction as deliberate craft choice for removing human agency from institutional processes.

### Move 5 — Present tense for immediacy \[Lane C both\]

Olmstead on Prejean: "She has decided to use the present tense, making the events immediate." Also on Beattie's ending. Shift to present tense at moments where the reader must feel co-present.

### Move 6 — Offstage elision (dense dramatic narrative) \[Lane A deai_removal\]

Olmstead on Snyder: "The trip to the emergency room, being there, and coming home all happen offstage." Skip narrating connective action; use a line break or time-marker to jump past it.

deai relevance: RLHF models fill every gap ("They rushed to the hospital, where the doctors..."). This move names elision as craft technique, not deficiency.

### Move 7 — Story-inside-story transition via voice texture \[Lane B tic_enrichment\]

Olmstead on Goyen: "The movement is gradual and inexorable, as if sliding under water." Shift into an interior story by first marking the sound of the storyteller's voice, then posing questions, then beginning the tale before the reader notices the transition.

### Move 8 — Memory rendered as conjuring (say / maybe / always) \[Lane B tic_enrichment\]

Olmstead on Baldwin: "He is seeing with his eyes closed, conjuring up the past." Use hedging words (say, maybe) not as weakness but as the texture of memory. Repeat framing phrases ("I always see her") to hold the scene in place.

\[esl_preserve\]: the use of "say" as a hedge/invitation is common in Chinese-English discourse ("say, around three o'clock"). Do not flag as informal or imprecise.

### Move 9 — Perceiving through another character's interpretation \[Lane C both\]

Olmstead on Bausch: "the reading of that nod is placed inside Jane's head." Instead of describing an expression directly, filter it through a perceiving character's interpretation.

### Move 10 — Conflicted mind (imagination vs. truth) \[Lane A deai_removal\]

Olmstead on Lott: "The conflict occurs within a contentious mind, a mind that has to face up to the differences between imagination and truth." Pair "I used to imagine" with "But the truth of the matter was."

deai relevance: RLHF models resolve internal conflict too quickly ("While he initially thought X, he soon realized Y"). This move holds the tension between versions.

### Move 11 — Decision as vision (extended metaphor) \[Lane B tic_enrichment\]

Olmstead on Cheever: "The decision becomes a vision." Render a character's decision through a sustained metaphor drawn from one domain (cartography, aviation, etc.).

### Move 12 — Revelation paired with opposite action \[Lane C both\]

Olmstead on Dubus: "He pairs opposite actions. The sleep is quiet and calm and dreamy, and the revelation is quick." Stage a realization at a transition between physical states (sleeping to waking, stillness to motion).

### Move 13 — Ending with declarative knowledge \[Lane A deai_removal\]

Olmstead on Ford: "Ford ends here on the tight urgency of something realized that must be said before such clarity of mind is lost." Character states what they know in plain, repeated syntactic structures ("It was about...").

deai relevance: RLHF models hedge conclusions with qualifiers ("Perhaps the most important lesson was..."). Ford's move is unhedged declaration.

### Move 14 — Gentle-verb fade-out \[Lane B tic_enrichment\]

Olmstead on Warren: "walk, watch, lift, will deaden and shall move. These are all very easy, gentle verbs." Close a section by downshifting verb energy — replace active/violent verbs with soft/ambient ones.

### Move 15 — Obsession rendered without labeling \[Lane A deai_removal\]

Olmstead on West: "West could have provided a psychological label for Miss Lonelyhearts, something from the vocabulary of therapists. But that wouldn't be writing. That would be labeling and analyzing." The move: show the obsession through its habitual manifestations (the shoes under the bed, the ties in the holder) and omit the conjunction "and" to make the list feel inexhaustible.

deai relevance: RLHF models reach for diagnostic/therapeutic vocabulary ("his OCD manifested as..."). This move replaces labeling with dramatization. Also note: dropping "and" from lists — a micro-craft move RLHF models never use.

### Move 16 — Dialogue as bridge from narrative \[Lane B tic_enrichment\]

Olmstead on Offutt: "Offutt makes a bridge to carry us into dialogue." Two methods: (a) describe the voice as a physical feature, then let the character speak; (b) have a physical action call forth speech. The move is about crafting the seam between narrative and spoken words.

tic relevance: structural borrow for transition craft. RLHF models switch to dialogue abruptly or with mechanical attribution ("she said, turning to face him").

### Move 17 — Sentence-lengthening for pace control in violence \[Lane B tic_enrichment\]

Olmstead on Crews: "he says it was impossible to follow what was happening." The move: in action/violence scenes, use commas, conjunctions, and qualifying clauses to make the sentence read more slowly than the action it conveys. "Note the commas and conjunctions, the qualifying of each and every action to slow the pace."

tic relevance: counterintuitive rhythm borrow — slowing prose during fast action. RLHF models do the opposite (short choppy sentences for all action).

### Move 18 — Unquoted dialogue differentiated by sound \[Lane B tic_enrichment\]

Olmstead on Paley: "Paley relies upon the way narrative and dialogue each have a distinct sound." The move: when dropping quotation marks, distinguish spoken words from narrative through contractions, exclamation marks, one-word commands, and name-shifts (Faith becomes Faithy).

tic relevance: voice texture at the punctuation level. Provides an alternative to the RLHF-standard "always quote dialogue" pattern.

### Move 19 — Imagination as crescendo \[Lane C both\]

Olmstead on Wilson: "It begins in a simple way, one thought leading to another and building in intensity, until finally it seems to have taken over her mind." The move: track a character's imagination escalating from one idle thought to full possession, then end with a physical marker of the internal state ("the knuckles of my hands were pale on the wheel").

### Move 20 — Gesture contradicting dialogue \[Lane A deai_removal\]

Olmstead on Hansen: "The actions that surround our words are as likely to betray them as support them." The move: use physical gestures to undercut or complicate what a character says. "Sister Aimée stares at her with honest interest and then she stands" — the standing cancels the honest interest.

deai relevance: RLHF models align gesture and speech (nodding while agreeing, frowning while disagreeing). This move introduces gesture-speech contradiction as a complexity tool.
