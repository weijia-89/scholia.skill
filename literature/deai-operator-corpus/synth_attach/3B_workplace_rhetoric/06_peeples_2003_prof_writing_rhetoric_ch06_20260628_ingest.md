## Core Arguments — Kostelnick (1988)

### The Spectrum of Visual Intensity

* All documents occupy a position on a continuum from low visual intensity (continuous prose) to high visual intensity (charts, diagrams, pictorial representations)
* Low end: conventional essay, novel — meaning embedded in continuous text blocks
* High end: data segmented into lists, matrices, charts — words become "thing-like" entities fixed spatially and graphically on the page
* Most business documents are "visual hybrids" — neither pure linguistic nor pure visual
* \[lane: tic_enrichment\] The spectrum concept provides vocabulary for calibrating voice-craft decisions about document texture: dense prose vs. surfaced structure is a rhetorical choice, not a formatting afterthought

### The 12-Cell Schema

Three coding modes crossed with four levels:

Coding modes:

* Alphanumeric/symbolic — letterforms, numbers, symbols, typeface selection
* Spatial — arrangement of signs across a plane, creating visual syntax
* Graphic — marks, lines, colors, textures, icons

Four levels:

* Intra-textual (cells 1-3): micro-level form — type style, size, weight, posture; local character spacing; punctuation and underlining
* Inter-textual (cells 4-6): serial/segmenting devices — headings, bullets, lists, matrices, flow charts; vertical/horizontal arrangement; cueing devices
* Extra-textual (cells 7-9): elements outside the main text — legends, captions, labels; data plots on axes; schematic lines/tones/colors; pictorial resolution
* Supra-textual (cells 10-12): macro-level document coherence — section titles, pagination, page headers; cohesion across pages; unifying graphic marks (logos, color, tabs)

Key principle: cells are interdependent. A cell-1 type size decision has supra-textual consequences. A cell-11 "one-page resume" constraint impinges on intra- and inter-textual choices.

### The Myth of Rhetorical Neutrality

* Information design movement (roots in Bauhaus functionalism) claimed to create rational, ideologically free sign systems
* Kostelnick argues this neutrality is impossible: visual elements carry meaning beyond information transfer
* Rhetorical infiltration occurs through taste, cultural values, technology constraints
* Even choosing a "neutral" configuration is a rhetorical act — acquiescing to someone else's data display is still choosing
* Technology shapes rhetoric: typewriter forced uniform spacing and initiated new conventions; handwritten vs. typed carries rhetorical weight regardless of content
* \[lane: both\] Phenomenological parallel to de-AI work: RLHF "clean" output presents itself as stylistically neutral while embodying specific commitments (hedging patterns, list-preference, tonal flattening). This parallels the information design neutrality myth at the level of observable effect, though the mechanism differs — Kostelnick describes intentional human design choices; RLHF output results from statistical optimization against preference data. The parallel is productive for recognition but should not be treated as identity. \[speculative — C-009\]

### Visual and Verbal as Holistic Process

* Visual design must be integrated into the writing process from planning stage, not bolted on after
* Visual and verbal rhetoric are interdependent: highly variegated inter-textual systems change sentence structure, increase parallelism and fragments, eliminate need for verbal transitions
* Data displays are "discovery tools, much like freewriting, zero drafting, and outlining"
* The writer-designer is a single role, not two roles separated by a handoff

## Core Arguments — Johnson (1998)

### The Problem with Instructions

* Instructions have dismal reputations despite ubiquity
* Root cause is not "bad writing" per se — readers can often comprehend syntax and vocabulary but cannot complete intended actions
* Instructions function as "masking devices for the complexity of systems and artifacts" — they make difficulty invisible, which paradoxically makes the instructions themselves look easy to write
* Instructions are "intruders into users' spaces" — humans want to "get going" with artifacts; documentation is perceived as a barrier to the goal, not a tool toward it

### Three Paradigms of Documentation Development

Johnson announces three approaches: "These three terms have been borrowed from various places, but the use of them as a taxonomic device to describe computer documentation development processes is my own invention." \[verified against primary text\]

System-centered (FULLY PRESENT in attached text):

* Artifact or system is the focus; documentation describes "thingness" of the technology
* Assumes information presence equals adequacy — "the fact that users have a text in front of them is enough"
* Places entire burden of comprehension on the user
* Expert-to-novice, top-down ideology; users positioned as passive receivers
* Historical origin: early computing where users were experts who needed no instructional texts

User-friendly (TRUNCATED — section not available in attached text):

* \[inferred from title, key terms, and Johnson's framing\] Improvement over system-centered but still technology-driven
* Readability and surface accessibility become goals
* Focus on making existing system descriptions more comprehensible rather than reconceiving what documentation should do
* \[lane: deai_removal\] The user-friendly paradigm maps directionally onto superficial de-AI that smooths surface markers without addressing structural RLHF patterns. Caveat: the analogy breaks on intent — user-friendly documentation at least improves surface comprehensibility for real users; surface de-AI may degrade voice without improving comprehensibility for anyone. \[speculative — C-010\]

User-centered (TRUNCATED — section not available in attached text):

* \[inferred from title, key terms list, and chapter subtitle\] Documentation development repositions the user as active participant
* Key Terms list confirms coverage of: iterative design (term 7)
* Chapter subtitle confirms coverage of: local knowledge, negotiation
* Substantive argument not verifiable from available text
* \[lane: tic_enrichment\] User-centered documentation theory provides structural warrant for voice-craft that preserves writer's situated knowledge rather than normalizing toward institutional register — but this application is a domain transfer from documentation to writing pedagogy. \[inferred — C-011\]

### The Literacy Framing Problem

* Williams et al. (1995) study: patients labeled "functionally illiterate" when they couldn't act on medical instructions at two large urban hospitals
* Williams et al. verified externally: JAMA 1995, PMID 7474271, cross-sectional survey of 2,659 patients using TOFHLA. Findings confirmed: 35.1% of English-speaking and 61.7% of Spanish-speaking patients had inadequate/marginal functional health literacy.
* Johnson's critique: the system defines literacy on its own terms, then blames users for failing; "Heaven forbid that the system should be changed to expose the black box!"
* Parallel to computer "illiteracy" — error message "type 15 occurred" provides no actionable information, but user is blamed for not understanding
* \[lane: both\] \[esl_preserve\] The burden-of-comprehension argument transfers to ESL contexts: labeling L1-influenced writing as deficient recapitulates system-centered assumptions. This is a domain transfer from technology documentation to writing pedagogy — the structural logic transfers (system blames user for comprehension failure) but the specific mechanisms differ. \[inferred — C-011\]

### Computer Documentation as Marginalized Text

* Johnson argues documentation has "seldom received the serious theoretical, historical, or general scholarly attention" given to other text types
* Computer documentation is "one of the most complicated forms of text we possess — both in terms of product and process"
