## 4\. Key Concepts and Frameworks

### 4a. PAR Stories (problem-action-results)

* Framework for answering behavioral interview questions
* Three components: describe the Problem, the Action taken, the Results achieved
* The chapter demonstrates via Holly/Allen dialogue that without probing, interviewees give shallow responses that fail to surface actual behavioral evidence
* \[Lane: tic_enrichment\] PAR is a narrative micro-structure — borrowable as a structural frame for concise professional anecdotes in cover letters and portfolio narratives. Structural borrow, not pastiche.

### 4b. Question Taxonomy (interviewer side)

| Type | Function | Signal quality |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Closed | Binary/short-answer confirmation | Low — yields almost no useful information (per Allen example) |
| Open | Elicits explanation, feelings, opinions | Medium — may still produce shallow answers |
| Probing | Digs beneath surface; requests specifics | High — forces concrete detail |
| Behavioral | Asks how respondent acted in specific past situation | Highest when paired with probing — surfaces actual competence evidence |

### 4c. OABC Pattern + FLATS (recommendation letters)

* OABC: Opening, Agenda, Body, Closing — letter-organization framework (defined in earlier chapters, referenced here)
* FLATS: \[referenced but not defined in visible export; likely defined in Chapter 2 or 3\] — document enhancement technique
* Bottom-up outlining process: brainstorm all items, select and categorize, then compose
* \[Lane: tic_enrichment\] The bottom-up outlining → OABC sequencing move is a transferable composition strategy for any professional letter genre.

### 4d. Thank-You Message Formula

Three-part structure:

1. Sincere thanks
2. Specific interview detail appreciated
3. Reaffirmation of interest

* Timing: within 24 hours
* Format: letter or email both acceptable
* \[Lane: both\] The specificity requirement in element 2 functions as an anti-generic move — forces the writer away from template language toward situated detail. See C-005 for external verification of generic language as an AI-detection marker.

### 4e. Salary Negotiation Framing

* Use external market data as logical justification (not emotional appeal)
* Competing-offer disclosure framed with preference signal: "I see a better fit with your company"
* Benefits comparison as systematic evaluation (co-pays, retirement, flex-time)
* \[Lane: both\] The negotiation framing advice models a craft move of anchoring assertions in external evidence rather than self-assessment — applicable to persuasive professional writing generally.

### 4f. Ethical Commitment Norm — ADVERSARIAL CORRECTION

The textbook claims: "you are legally obligated to keep your commitment" (Q-010).

This claim is wrong under US at-will employment law. External verification (FindLaw 2026, National Law Review, LegalClarity) establishes:

* Most US employment is at-will; a standard offer letter does not create a binding employment contract
* Candidates can withdraw acceptance before the start date without legal penalty in most cases
* Promissory estoppel may apply only where the employer reasonably relied on the acceptance to their detriment (e.g., the candidate caused them to reject other finalists, incur relocation costs)
* The textbook conflates moral norm with legal obligation without citing case law or jurisdiction

The reputation-damage claim (backing out harms your reputation and your school's) is a reasonable social observation but is presented as universal rather than contextual. See C-004 in Section 11 — claim tagged WRONG.

## 9\. Lane-Tagged Takeaways

### deai_removal

* The anti-generic specificity requirement in thank-you messages (Q-006) maps to RLHF-residue detection via C-007 \[inferred\]: requiring situated detail structurally opposes the generic-language pattern identified as the strongest AI-detection marker (C-005 \[verified\]).
* PAR structure enforces concrete, experience-grounded language that resists the abstract-hedging patterns common in AI-generated text. This is an \[inferred\] connection — no study directly tests PAR answers against AI-detection classifiers.

### tic_enrichment

* PAR as narrative micro-structure: borrowable for portfolio narratives, case-study writeups, and professional contexts requiring concise demonstration of competence through story. Supported by meta-analytic evidence that past-behavior questions predict job performance (C-001 \[verified\]).
* Bottom-up outlining → OABC sequencing: a composing-process move transferable to proposal writing, recommendation requests, and structured professional correspondence.
* Market-data anchoring: using external evidence rather than self-assessment as the justification frame — transferable to any persuasive professional document.
* Question escalation ladder (closed → open → probing → behavioral): transferable to user-research interview design and stakeholder-interview preparation.

### esl_preserve

* The chapter's PAR framework is structure-focused, not idiom-focused. It does not require "native-sounding" language — it requires organized content (problem, action, results). This structural emphasis is inherently ESL-compatible.
* \[esl_preserve\] The sample interview answers (Allen example) model informal spoken English but are not presented as the target register for written documents. No "sound more native" inference should be drawn from the dialogue examples.

### 11C. Failure Modes (per verified/inferred claim)

* C-001 \[verified\]: The meta-analyses measure structured interviews broadly, not PAR specifically. PAR is one format within past-behavior questioning. The validity coefficient (.63) applies to the question type + scoring method, not to the candidate's use of PAR structure. Baker's implicit claim (PAR makes answers better) is a different claim than "behavioral questions predict performance."
* C-002 \[inferred\]: The surveys (TopResume, Accountemps) measure stated preference, not actual hiring behavior. Social desirability bias likely inflates the percentage who say thank-you notes "matter." The staffing firms have commercial interest in selling interview-prep services. No study measures the marginal causal effect of a thank-you note on getting hired.
* C-005 \[verified\]: The Xu and Zubiaga study used Llama-7B, not production-scale models (GPT-4, Claude). Generalizability to current frontier models is uncertain. The RAG-XAI study tested peer-review text, not employment correspondence. Domain transfer is assumed, not demonstrated.
* C-007 \[inferred\]: The mapping from "anti-generic specificity" (etiquette advice) to "de-AI signal removal" (computational detection) is the ingest author's analogy. No study tests whether following Baker's thank-you formula reduces AI-detection scores on employment correspondence.
* C-008 \[verified\]: "Structured" is defined variously across the meta-analytic literature. Schmidt and Hunter's definition encompasses standardized questions + rating scales. Baker's chapter does not prescribe standardized rating scales; Baker's "structured" is informal.

### 11D. Three Strongest Hostile-Expert Objections

Objection 1: The ingest treats Baker's PAR framework as having meta-analytic support, but the meta-analyses (Taylor and Small 2002, Schmidt and Hunter 1998) study the interviewer's question format and scoring method — not the candidate's answer structure. No study demonstrates that candidates who use PAR get hired at higher rates than candidates who give unstructured but detailed answers. The ingest conflates interviewer-side evidence with candidate-side advice.

Response: Objection succeeds partially. C-001 confidence capped at 70. The ingest now distinguishes between "behavioral questions have predictive validity" (verified) and "PAR format makes candidate answers better" (speculative — Baker's claim, not externally validated).

Objection 2: The de-AI mapping (C-007) is a post-hoc analogy the ingest author constructed, not a finding from the textbook or from AI-detection research. Generic thank-you language being a detectable AI pattern does not mean that following Baker's thank-you formula is an effective de-AI intervention. The ingest risks dressing up a plausible analogy as a verified craft move.

Response: Objection succeeds. C-007 remains \[inferred\] and cannot be promoted. The skill incorporation table retains "anti-generic specificity" as KEEP but with explicit caveat that the de-AI mapping is inferential.

Objection 3: The textbook's functional-resume recommendation is not merely outdated — it may actively harm career changers who follow it. If 62% of recruiters view functional resumes as red flags and ATS parsing fails at \~35% rates, then Baker's advice produces worse outcomes than no advice. The ingest should flag this as a pedagogical hazard, not merely a "contested convention."

Response: Objection succeeds. C-003 downgraded to \[speculative\] with OVERSTATED tag. The skill incorporation table does not include functional-resume advice (it was never a craft move in the table, but the confidence score section now reflects the hazard).

The textbook's empirical claims (thank-you notes differentiate, functional resumes suit changers, offer acceptance is legally binding) range from weakly supported to demonstrably false.
