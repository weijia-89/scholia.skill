## Scope and Coverage Attestation

* Domain: AI-generated text detection in rehabilitation/medical academic writing
* Model tested: GPT-3.5-Turbo (July 2023 vintage)
* Paraphrase tool: Wordtune
* Corpus: 50 original rehabilitation articles from Nature, Lancet, JAMA, BMJ (2013-2023); 50 ChatGPT-fabricated counterparts; 50 AI-rephrased versions
* Sections fabricated: Introduction, Discussion, Conclusion only (Methods and Results kept from originals)
* Detectors tested: Originality.ai, Turnitin, ZeroGPT, GPTZero, Content at Scale, GPT-2 Output Detector
* Human reviewers: 2 student reviewers, 2 professorial reviewers (physiotherapy background)
* Language: English only
* Coverage attestation: Full-text paper read and verified against publisher version (Springer, 200 OK, open access). No supplementary appendices available in export. Claims extracted from main body only.
* Confidence ceiling: Cross-sectional study design caps all claims at max 60. Additional quality deductions applied where noted (underpowered N, unreplicated, forced-choice instrument limitations).

## Key Findings / Claims Register

### C-001: Originality.ai achieved 100% detection accuracy on both ChatGPT-generated and AI-rephrased texts

* Source: Results section, Fig 2a
* Epistemic tag: \[verified:primary-data\] — but OVERSTATED for generalization
* Calibration: \[c1=50, c2=35, c3=40\] FINAL=40
* Prior FINAL: 70. Reduced to 40 after adversarial review.
* Failure mode: The 100% figure is bound to GPT-3.5-Turbo output + Wordtune paraphrasing + rehabilitation domain + N=50. Independent subsequent studies falsify generalizability:
  * Springer Nature 2026 study (s40979-026-00213-1): Originality overall accuracy 0.69, macro-average F1 below 0.55 \[verified: Gate A 200 OK, Gate B CLAIM IN SOURCE\]
  * GPTZero benchmark (3,000 samples): Originality accuracy 83.0%, false positive rate 4.79% \[verified: Gate A 200 OK, Gate B CLAIM IN SOURCE — note: source is GPTZero (competitor), COI flag\]
  * Supwriter independent test: Originality overall 83.4%, Claude detection only 72-75%, false positive rate 5% \[verified: Gate A 200 OK, Gate B CLAIM IN SOURCE — note: review site, Tier 3\]
* Verdict: The 100% figure is accurate for the specific experimental conditions reported. It does not generalize. Any downstream use of this claim must scope it to GPT-3.5 + Wordtune + rehab domain + N=50.

### C-002: Paraphrasing reduced AI scores across all detectors except Originality.ai

* Source: Results section, Fig 4a
* Epistemic tag: \[verified:primary-data\]
* Calibration: \[c1=55, c2=55, c3=50\] FINAL=55
* Prior FINAL: 75. Reduced to 55 (cross-sectional ceiling 60, minus quality deduction for single paraphraser).
* Failure mode: Only Wordtune tested. Other paraphrasers (Quillbot, human editing, GPT-4 self-revision) may produce different evasion profiles.
* Convergent evidence: Weber-Wulff et al. 2023 also found content obfuscation degrades detector performance \[verified: Gate A 200 OK, Gate B CLAIM IN SOURCE\]. Direction of effect is robust; magnitude is Wordtune-specific.

### C-003: Turnitin showed 0% false positive rate on human-written articles but only 30% detection of AI-rephrased articles

* Source: Results section, Fig 2a/b
* Epistemic tag: \[verified:primary-data\]
* Calibration: \[c1=55, c2=50, c3=50\] FINAL=50
* Prior FINAL: 75. Reduced to 50 (cross-sectional ceiling; N=50; Turnitin has updated since August 2023).
* Failure mode: Turnitin's AI detection module has been updated multiple times since data collection. The 30% figure is stale. The 0% false positive rate on N=50 high-quality journal articles does not predict performance on typical student or ESL submissions.

### C-004: Professorial reviewers detected 96-100% of AI-rephrased articles but misclassified 12% of human-written articles as AI-generated

* Source: Results section, Table 1, Fig 2c/d
* Epistemic tag: \[verified:primary-data\]
* Calibration: \[c1=50, c2=45, c3=45\] FINAL=45
* Prior FINAL: 65. Reduced to 45 (N=2 reviewers is critically underpowered; cross-sectional ceiling minus quality deduction).
* Failure mode: Two reviewers cannot establish stable detection rates. The 12% false positive rate is directionally important for ESL concern but the exact value has wide confidence intervals on N=2.
* ESL relevance: The paper reports that misclassified human articles were "deemed to be incoherent and/or lack vocabulary diversity" (Q-010 area). Both criteria overlap with ESL writing characteristics. \[esl_preserve:critical\]

### C-005: Student reviewers only identified 76% of AI-rephrased articles on average

* Source: Results section, Table 1
* Epistemic tag: \[verified:primary-data\]
* Calibration: \[c1=50, c2=45, c3=45\] FINAL=45
* Prior FINAL: 65. Reduced to 45 (N=2 student reviewers; inconsistency between the two students noted in Limitations section).

### C-006: Top reasons reviewers flagged articles as AI-generated: incoherence (34.36%), grammatical errors (20.26%), insufficient evidence (16.15%)

* Source: Results section
* Epistemic tag: \[verified:primary-data\] — but OVERSTATED as stable frequency data
* Calibration: \[c1=45, c2=40, c3=45\] FINAL=45
* Prior FINAL: 70. Reduced to 45 after identifying two compounding methodological problems:
* Problem 1 — Forced-choice instrument: Reviewers selected "top three reasons" from a pre-defined list (coherence, creativity, evidence-based, grammatical errors, vocabulary diversity) with option for free-text additions. The frequencies reflect the instrument's option menu as much as detection cognition. A JOWR 2025 paper ("It's Giving AI") explicitly critiques this approach, arguing reviewers rely on "felt sense" that forced-choice items cannot capture. \[verified: Gate A 200 OK, Gate B CLAIM IN SOURCE\]
* Problem 2 — Inter-rater reliability on reasons: Cohen's kappa for primary reason = 0.211 (95% CI 0.011-0.411). This falls in the "slight-to-fair" range per Landis-Koch benchmarks \[verified: PMC 2014 kappa interpretation guide, Gate A 200 OK, Gate B CLAIM IN SOURCE\]. At this reliability level, the specific frequency percentages (34.36% vs. 20.26% vs. 16.15%) carry false precision. The ranking order (incoherence > grammar > evidence) is probably directionally real but the exact percentages are unreliable.
* Verdict: The tells exist and were reported by reviewers. The frequency ranking is approximate. The false precision of the percentages should not drive craft-move prioritization.

### C-007: GPTZero perplexity scores performed worse than chance (AUROC 0.312) at discriminating human vs AI text

* Source: Results section, Fig 3b
* Epistemic tag: \[verified:primary-data\]
* Calibration: \[c1=55, c2=55, c3=50\] FINAL=55
* Prior FINAL: 75. Reduced to 55 (cross-sectional ceiling; single dataset).
* Failure mode: This tests GPTZero's perplexity score specifically, not perplexity as a theoretical construct. Other implementations of perplexity-based detection may perform differently. The claim is about this tool's implementation, not about perplexity in general.
* Convergent evidence: Liang et al. 2023 also found perplexity correlates with linguistic sophistication rather than AI authorship \[verified\]. Direction is robust.

### C-008: AI content detectors penalize non-native English writers with limited linguistic expressions due to simplified wording

* Source: Discussion section of Liu et al., citing Liang et al. 2023
* Epistemic tag: \[verified:independent-source\] — UPGRADED from \[inferred:secondary-cite\]
* Calibration: \[c1=75, c2=70, c3=70\] FINAL=70
* Prior FINAL: 70 (unchanged, but epistemic tag upgraded and confidence now grounded in independent verification rather than secondary citation).
* Independent verification: Liang et al. 2023 (Patterns 4(7):100779) reports 61.3% average false positive rate on 91 TOEFL essays from non-native Chinese writers across seven GPT detectors. All detectors unanimously flagged 19.8% of human-written TOEFL essays as AI-generated. 97.8% were flagged by at least one detector. \[verified: Gate A 200 OK via both ScienceDirect and PMC, Gate B CLAIM IN SOURCE — specific numbers confirmed across multiple source views\]
* Note: Liang et al. tested different detectors than Liu et al. (seven detectors vs. six, with partial overlap). The ESL bias finding is convergent across both studies despite different detector sets and corpora.
* Ceiling exception: This claim draws on Liang et al. as an independent study (not just the Liu cross-sectional design), and the Liang finding has been replicated/cited across Stanford HAI, PMC, and multiple secondary analyses. Ceiling raised from cross-sectional 60 to 70 based on convergent multi-study evidence.

### C-009: No human-written articles were simultaneously misclassified by both AI detectors and professorial reviewers

* Source: Discussion section
* Epistemic tag: \[verified:primary-data\]
* Calibration: \[c1=50, c2=40, c3=40\] FINAL=40
* Prior FINAL: 65. Reduced to 40 (N=50 articles, N=2 professorial reviewers, N=6 detectors all tested on GPT-3.5 output only; this is a negative finding on a small sample — absence of evidence, not evidence of absence).
* Failure mode: With N=50 and only 2 reviewers, the probability of observing at least one dual-misclassification is low even under random assignment. The finding is underpowered to support the policy conclusion the authors draw from it.

### Key finding for deai_removal lane

The ingest's prior framing claimed this paper "empirically separates structural from surface tells." That framing is operator-constructed, not author-argued. Retagged: \[inferred:operator-constructed\].

The underlying observation remains directionally useful: some reviewer-identified tells (incoherence, insufficient evidence, redundancy) operate at discourse/structure level and do not collide with ESL syntax, while others (grammatical errors, vocabulary diversity) operate at surface-linguistic level and do collide with ESL syntax per Liang et al. 2023 \[verified\].

The distinction is real but the evidence base is weaker than initially presented:

* The tell frequencies have low inter-rater reliability (kappa 0.211)
* The structural tells are confounded with the fabrication method (section-independent generation)
* The surface tells are confounded with ESL writing features (verified independently via Liang et al.)
* No evidence that fixing these tells would change detector output or human reviewer assessment

The craft moves (CM-001 through CM-004) survive as general writing-quality improvements that happen to overlap with AI-flagged features. They are not validated as deai-specific interventions.

Step 2: Three strongest hostile expert objections

Objection 1: "The tell frequencies are artifacts of the forced-choice instrument, not empirical findings about AI detection."

Response: Accepted. The reviewers chose from a pre-defined list. The kappa of 0.211 on reasons means the two professorial reviewers rarely agreed on why they flagged an article, even when they agreed on whether to flag it (kappa 0.819 on binary decision). The frequency percentages cannot be treated as stable empirical data. Affected claims: C-006 downgraded from FINAL=70 to FINAL=45. Craft-move prioritization based on these frequencies is unreliable. All four craft moves retagged as "general quality moves" rather than "empirically validated deai signals."

Objection 2: "The structural tells (incoherence, evidence-thinness) are artifacts of the fabrication method (section-independent generation), not intrinsic AI markers. GPT-4/Claude producing sections with access to prior context would not exhibit these tells."

Response: Accepted. The study's prompting method ("Please write a convincing scientific introduction on the topic of \[original topic\]" and "Please critically discuss the methods and results below") generated each section without access to other generated sections. Incoherence between sections is a predictable artifact of this pipeline, not a finding about AI writing in general. A more capable model with multi-section prompting would produce fewer of these tells. This does not eliminate the craft moves' value as quality improvements, but it severs the causal link between "AI-generated" and "exhibits these tells." The tells are method-specific, not model-intrinsic.

Objection 3: "The ESL bias claim is imported from Liang et al. 2023, which tested different detectors on TOEFL essays — a different domain, genre, and text type than medical articles. The cross-domain transfer is assumed, not demonstrated."

Response: Partially accepted. Liang et al. tested seven detectors (including GPTZero, which overlaps with Liu et al.'s detector set) on TOEFL essays. Liu et al. tested six detectors on medical articles. The ESL bias mechanism (detectors penalizing low lexical diversity) is theoretically domain-general, and Liang et al.'s 61.3% false positive rate on non-native writing is striking. However, the specific magnitude of ESL bias on medical-domain text with medical-domain detectors is \[unknown\]. The kill-list entries for grammar/vocabulary tells remain justified because the collision direction is confirmed even if the magnitude is uncertain. C-008 confidence capped at 70 (multi-study convergence) rather than lowered further, but the domain-transfer caveat is now explicit.
